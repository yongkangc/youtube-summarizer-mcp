# The Truth About Investing at All-Time Highs

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WEClhtPD5Ko

## TL;DR

Ben Felix argues all-time highs are not a reason to sell or delay investing. Index levels naturally rise over time because dividends, inflation, and positive expected equity returns push them upward. Valuations matter more than index highs, but valuation timing is noisy enough that most investors should stay invested and stick to plan.

## Key Insights

- Fear of all-time highs is often gambler’s fallacy.
  - Investors feel like many positive returns must be followed by bad returns.
  - But short-term stock returns are close to random, with some momentum.
  - A market hitting new highs does not by itself predict bad future returns.

- Index levels are less meaningful than media coverage suggests.
  - S&P 500 and similar indices have arbitrary base levels.
  - Most commonly cited indices are price-only, excluding dividends.
  - Total return indices, including reinvested dividends, are better representation of investor experience.
  - Price-only framing makes markets look worse after dividends are paid, even though investors received cash.

- Nominal highs should be expected.
  - Index levels are nominal, so inflation alone pushes them upward.
  - Stocks also have positive expected long-term returns.
  - Therefore new highs should be normal, not surprising.

- Developed-market data says all-time highs are common.
  - Ben studies 10 developed markets plus world market from 1970 to May 2026 using monthly total returns.
  - Average individual-country market: all-time highs in about 20% of months.
  - US: about 30% of months.
  - Canada: about 23% of months.
  - Italy: about 9%, lowest in sample.
  - World index: about 31% of months, helped by diversification.

- Highs cluster because momentum exists.
  - All-time highs are often followed by more all-time highs.
  - Bad/volatile periods also cluster.
  - This is consistent with short-term momentum, not mean-reversion panic.

- Returns after all-time highs are not especially bad.
  - For US market, returns after all-time highs were higher than other months at 1-, 3-, and 5-year horizons.
  - At 10 years, returns were similar but slightly lower.
  - Canada and other countries are mixed short-term but broadly similar long-term.
  - Across markets, 1-year returns after highs tend to be higher, consistent with momentum.

- Valuation is different from index level.
  - High all-time highs often coincide with high valuations, but they are not same concept.
  - Shiller CAPE measures stock prices relative to smoothed earnings.
  - Higher valuations imply lower expected future returns in theory because investors are paying more for future cash flows.

- Valuations have signal, but noisy signal.
  - Ben studies 10 developed markets from 1982 to 2024, sorting future 10-year returns by starting CAPE.
  - Higher starting CAPE generally means lower average future 10-year returns.
  - But distribution is wide: high valuations can still be followed by good returns.
  - This makes valuation-based market timing difficult.

- US-only doom can overstate risk.
  - US CAPE above 40 has historically preceded poor US returns in several periods.
  - But broader country sample shows more varied outcomes.
  - One-country history is too narrow for hard timing rules.

## Practical Takeaways

- Do not sell just because market hits all-time high.
- Do not delay investing solely because index level looks high.
- Valuation can inform expected return assumptions, but not precise timing.
- Market timing requires being right twice: when to exit and when to re-enter.
- For most investors, best move is stay in seat and follow long-term asset-allocation plan.

## Investor Read-Through

- For long-term allocators: all-time highs are not bearish signal; they are normal feature of compounding assets.
- For tactical traders: valuation is a slow, noisy prior, not a trigger.
- For YK-style risk process: distinguish index-level fear from actual underwriting question — expected return, valuation, earnings growth, rates, positioning, and liquidity.
- Main caveat: high valuations can lower 10-year expected returns, but using that to time exits often destroys more value than it saves.

## One-Line Summary

All-time highs are normal and historically not a sell signal; valuations matter more, but they are too noisy for most investors to time markets reliably.
