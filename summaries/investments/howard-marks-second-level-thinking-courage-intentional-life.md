# Howard Marks: Second-Level Thinking, Courage and Living Intentionally

## TLDR

Howard Marks connects investing and life through one discipline: act intentionally while admitting uncertainty. Superior investment results require a correct variant perception, but conviction should never become certainty. His personal regret is spending roughly his first 25 professional years making career choices passively and haphazardly; his advice is to choose work and relationships around one's own strengths, values and happiness rather than external expectations.

## AI and human judgment

Marks upgraded his view of AI after seeing its autonomy, contextual awareness, humour and ability to discuss its own limitations. Earlier technologies were tools; AI may increasingly act independently.

He remains uncertain whether AI can reproduce judgment when:

- no historical pattern exists;
- decisions depend mostly on supposition;
- character or trust feels wrong despite weak explicit evidence;
- future probability distributions differ from prior experience.

AI may become better than humans at most repeatable analysis. Remaining human edge would therefore lie in genuinely novel situations, calibrated judgment and understanding people.

## Second-level thinking

Marks defines investment edge clearly:

> To outperform, one must see something different from consensus, bet on it and be right.

Variant perception may concern:

- business quality;
- growth rate;
- earnings power;
- appropriate valuation multiple;
- market psychology.

Being different is insufficient. Consensus can be correct. Marks doubts that genuine insight can be taught directly, although investors can learn why it matters and improve their process.

**Theory of edge:** correctly identify where consensus expectations misprice the probability distribution, then size the position so being early or wrong does not destroy the portfolio.

## Oaktree during 2008

Oaktree anticipated distress and raised an **$11 billion** fund before Lehman failed. Its previous largest distressed fund had been approximately **$2.5 billion**.

After Lehman's collapse, Bruce Karsh deployed roughly:

- **$450 million per week**;
- for **15 weeks**;
- approximately **$7 billion** total.

Their reasoning was asymmetric:

- If the financial system collapsed and they invested, investment outcomes would no longer matter much.
- If the system survived and they failed to invest, they would have neglected their mandate.

They also bought debt with substantial valuation protection: some positions could break even even if businesses formerly acquired by private equity fell to roughly one-quarter or one-fifth of their acquisition values.

Marks stresses they were not confident. Terrible news affected them too. Probabilistic investors act with trepidation because they know they may be wrong.

> If you wait until there is nothing to fear, the opportunity has probably passed.

## Build the ark before the flood

Crisis capital must be raised before panic begins. Once headlines become catastrophic, investors will not commit fresh capital.

Oaktree earned credibility by sometimes raising smaller successor funds after strong performance, because better past returns often meant assets had appreciated and opportunity had weakened. Speaking against its own fundraising interest demonstrated alignment.

Durable crisis edge requires:

- capital secured in advance;
- historical credibility;
- dry powder;
- mandate clarity;
- willingness to deploy when emotion resists;
- valuation protection rather than blind contrarianism.

## Partnership

Marks and Bruce Karsh had been partners for **39 years**. Their framework:

1. **Shared values** — ethics, risk tolerance and financial ambition must align.
2. **Complementary skills** — each partner must contribute something the other cannot or does not want to do.
3. **Mutual respect** — intellectual disagreement without personal conflict.
4. **Appreciation** — recognize value created by the partner's different role.
5. **No financial maximization** — reducing money-status competition helps preserve trust.

Marks handled communication and fundraising; Karsh managed distressed investments. Buffett and Munger represented a similar combination of affection, respect and complementary thinking. Munger's key strategic contribution was moving Buffett from cheap "cigar butts" toward great businesses at good prices.

## Parenting and choice

Marks recommends giving children strong support without needing to prove parental superiority. When several choices are acceptable, let children decide. They gain experience making both correct and incorrect choices.

His broader lesson: tolerate recoverable mistakes so judgment can compound.

## The 25-year regret

Marks says his early career decisions lacked intention:

- he joined Citi because a prior summer there had gone well;
- moved from equities after unsuccessful work;
- entered bonds because he was reassigned;
- moved to California partly for sunshine and palm trees;
- encountered Michael Milken and high-yield bonds largely through luck.

The outcomes were excellent, but process was not. He distinguishes fortunate outcome from good decision-making.

His career test:

- play to strengths;
- avoid structural weaknesses;
- choose work that makes you happy;
- do not let friends, parents or society choose your scorecard.

Christopher Morley's line captures it:

> There is only one success: to live your life your own way.

## Humility and sizing

Useful opening for any thesis: **"I could be wrong, but…"**

Certainty is dangerous because an 80/20 proposition still produces the adverse outcome 20% of the time. Betting as though probability were 100% creates ruin.

Separate:

- belief strong enough to act;
- humility strong enough to size safely;
- evidence strong enough to update;
- randomness strong enough to prevent over-crediting oneself.

Marks recommends:

- *A Short History of Financial Euphoria* by John Kenneth Galbraith;
- *Fooled by Randomness* by Nassim Nicholas Taleb.

Short-term performance cannot reliably distinguish skill from luck.

## YK read

This maps directly onto one-man-hedge-fund process:

- Variant perception is not edge until mechanism, evidence and price differ from consensus.
- Raise liquidity and define mandate before crisis; cannot improvise dry powder during panic.
- Fear is compatible with good decisions. Absence of fear may signal unrecognized risk.
- Every thesis should include `I could be wrong`, probability, sizing and invalidation.
- Internal scorecard matters: excellent outcomes reached through accidental choices do not excuse weak process.
- Family and durable partnerships compound alongside capital; financial maximization can damage more valuable assets.

## Bottom line

Marks's durable lesson is not merely contrarianism. Prepare before crisis, demand valuation protection, act despite uncertainty, preserve survival, and choose life deliberately rather than mistaking lucky outcomes or external approval for good process.
