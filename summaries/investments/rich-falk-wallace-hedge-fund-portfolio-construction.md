# Rich Falk-Wallace: Hedge Fund Failure, Alpha and Portfolio Construction

## TL;DR

A PM’s job is not merely finding good ideas. It is delivering return above freely available beta for an allocator, without leaking performance through factor exposure, correlation, bad sizing or poor process. Rich claims roughly **90% of PM/hedge-fund failures** come from portfolio construction and sizing rather than inability to generate ideas.

Theory of edge: hold many genuinely idiosyncratic bets where research has positive alpha hit rate; minimize unrewarded factor/correlation exposure so returns reflect skill rather than market beta.

## PM as product manager

A hedge fund sells a financial product:

- Return = revenue.
- Volatility/risk = cost.
- Allocator pays fees only for return beyond cheap alternatives such as index beta.

Therefore a PM must both generate alpha and prevent unintended risk from consuming it.

## How alpha is generated

A thesis must be both **different and correct**. Rich rejects single-variable debates:

- valuation versus earnings beats: both matter;
- macro versus company fundamentals: both matter;
- data advantage versus judgment: both matter.

Modern alpha often comes from interaction between layers:

1. differentiated/refined data;
2. correct business or industry heuristic;
3. understanding macro/factor environment;
4. translating these into valuation and market expectations;
5. sizing/expression that preserves payoff.

Alpha is competitive; advantage at only one layer is usually insufficient.

## Hit rate, slugging and correlation

There are two broad ways to sustain strong portfolio performance:

- extraordinary hit rate/slugging from concentrated bets; or
- broad positive-alpha hit rate with low correlation.

Concentration requires enormous proof of skill. Across many PMs, Rich says exceptional sizing or slugging skill is rarer than modest but repeatable positive alpha hit rate.

Preferred scalable model:

- maximize breadth of ideas where skill exists;
- maintain positive average alpha hit rate;
- reduce ex-ante correlation and factor overlap.

Breadth without edge only adds noise.

## Why correlation matters

Correlated positions replicate a common factor. That pulls portfolio Sharpe toward factor Sharpe—roughly market Sharpe of about **0.5–0.6** in his framing—which investor can obtain cheaply.

A “market-neutral” collection of PMs can still hide common exposure:

- all long high-beta technology;
- all exposed to momentum, revisions or crowding;
- sector-neutral individually but collectively loaded on same theme.

Risk must be aggregated across whole book, not checked pod by pod.

Forward-looking correlation matters more than historical labels. Factors can switch on/off, crowding can unwind and crisis correlation can rise.

## Fundamental plus systematic thinking

Risk decomposition is not enemy of fundamental investing. It helps separate:

- benchmark/factor-driven return;
- idiosyncratic company return;
- actual thesis success.

Instead of asking “Why did Microsoft rise?”, ask why it deviated from correct benchmark and which KPIs explained idiosyncratic move.

This prevents claiming skill for sector beta. Example: semiconductor PM up during broad semiconductor rally cannot be evaluated without decomposing common factor.

## Portfolio-construction process

Useful controls:

- hard limits on market, sector, momentum, crowding, revision and thematic factors;
- idea-level conviction and price targets captured before outcome;
- post-trade attribution: thesis, factor, sizing and timing;
- compare high-conviction versus low-conviction results;
- test whether fine-grained rank ordering adds value or simple buckets work better;
- inspect whether largest positions actually outperform smaller ones;
- measure correlation at portfolio and center-book level.

Risk system should expose hidden beta, not mechanically eliminate every factor. A factor bet may be valid if PM has explicit edge and expected payoff justifies Sharpe drag.

## Accountability and analyst development

Best firms increasingly turn analysts into capital allocators earlier:

- mock portfolios;
- long/short idea trackers;
- recorded conviction and targets;
- performance decomposition;
- rapid feedback loops.

Purpose: prevent hindsight rewriting—“I said buy before rise, but also said sell before fall.” Store decision before outcome.

Mock books are tests, not permanent verdicts. They reveal strengths, weaknesses and whether outcomes reflect skill or chance.

Manual modeling/data work declines with AI. Analyst value shifts toward:

- differentiated research;
- synthesis;
- judgment;
- idea generation;
- capital allocation.

Junior analysts are not obsolete, but technology increases dispersion. Top talent reaches senior-level knowledge faster and gains more leverage.

## AI and tooling

AI can accelerate models, trackers, data access, process integration and mock books. Rich expects AI agents/fundamental-replicating quant systems to become one strategy class, not replace all human fundamental investing.

Human bottleneck remains forming differentiated judgment across finite coverage universe. One investor cannot understand every stock with edge, even with better tools.

## Industry structure

Likely trends:

- more beta-one extension products such as 130/30;
- more beta-zero/market-neutral products because skill is easier to audit;
- continued analytical decomposition of performance;
- more successful multi-manager PMs launching specialist funds;
- SMA capital and better tooling lower startup friction;
- large multi-strategy firms retain scale advantages in corporate access, infrastructure and procurement.

Investment industry remains fragmented because alpha capacity and human attention limit winner-take-all economics.

## New-fund failure mode

Biggest operating mistake: trying to build too many things at once.

- Very large firms can evaluate build-versus-buy and fund multiple verticals.
- Small firms know they lack bandwidth and buy best tools.
- Mid-sized/new launches can overbuild infrastructure, add asset classes and distract from stock picking.

Rule: build only where no strong external product exists. Buy commodity infrastructure. Preserve attention for unique edge.

## One-man hedge-fund read-through

For YK:

1. Define product: return target, acceptable drawdown, beta and liquidity.
2. Record thesis, expected catalyst, invalidation, target and confidence before entry.
3. Track factor map: AI beta, semis, duration, crypto, commodities, USD and short volatility.
4. Measure idea hit rate separately from factor tailwind.
5. Size from edge × uncertainty × correlation × ruin risk—not conviction emotion.
6. Audit whether strongest conviction actually deserves larger size.
7. Keep enough independent bets, but no forced breadth without edge.
8. Monthly attribution: thesis alpha, market beta, factor beta, sizing, timing, options carry and execution.
9. Buy commodity tooling; build only proprietary decision workflow.
10. Focus. Do not become researcher, infra company and ten-strategy fund simultaneously.

## Caveats

- “90% of failures” is guest judgment, not demonstrated dataset.
- Low measured historical correlation can fail during stress.
- Factor models are model-dependent and can misclassify genuine thesis exposure.
- Sharpe is incomplete: ignores liquidity, path, convexity and tail severity.
- More breadth helps only when each additional idea has positive expected alpha.

## One-line summary

Finding good ideas creates potential alpha; portfolio construction, correlation control and honest attribution determine whether investor keeps it.
