# Catching Up With Power Investors Howard Marks and Bruce Flatt | At Barron's

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZPpcUUe9--k

## TLDR

Howard Marks says markets are optimism-driven: credit is generous, IPO appetite is back, and expected forward returns should be lower unless investors prepare for less optimistic conditions. Bruce Flatt is more constructive: Brookfield sees real cash-flow opportunities in infrastructure, power, data centers, and AI factories because demand is huge but supply is hard to build.

## Key points

- Brookfield fully absorbed Oaktree after a long test period. Flatt frames the logic as giving Brookfield scaled credit capability alongside its global infrastructure/equity businesses.

- Marks says Oaktree sold because Brookfield respected its decentralized investment process while giving it more resources and proprietary capital support.

- Flatt says Marks’ main value to Brookfield is not day-to-day deal decisions, but capital-market judgment: culture, ethos, when to invest heavily, when to slow down.

- Marks’ market view: since late 2022, optimism has dominated. Optimism enables big IPOs and higher prices vs intrinsic value, which usually means lower future returns.

- His practical warning: even if you participate in upside, part of you should constantly ask, “how do we prepare for less optimistic times?”

- Credit-cycle view: credit has had an unusually long good run since 2009. Lending standards loosen when lenders compete, FOMO rises, and borrowers get easy money. Marks thinks conditions are generous now, so discipline matters.

- Rates: Marks sees no reason to cut. Economy does not need stimulus, and easier policy risks rekindling inflation. He hints there are more reasons to raise than cut, but avoids telling the Fed what to do.

- Flatt’s equity view: broad indices may look expensive because of AI/IPO excitement, but many cash-flowing businesses still trade at low multiples. Brookfield has been taking companies private and expects recent/future deals to earn attractive returns over time.

- AI/electrification: Flatt is bullish on the real buildout. Brookfield sees demand for power, data centers, and “AI factories” everywhere. His key point: supply will undershoot expectations because siting power, connecting grids, building facilities, procuring chips, and financing $20–250B projects is hard.

- He is not worried about an immediate AI-infra bubble in the assets Brookfield builds because they lease to sovereigns and top companies, with real demand and hard physical constraints.

- Marks on software/private-credit distress: he sees worry about software debt, but not yet a spike in actual distress. His view: market concern may be excessive; some companies will have case-by-case problems, but revenues/debt service have not collapsed broadly.

- Private markets: Marks says private markets grew partly because leverage worked well during falling rates and because recent good times made investors forget the downside of illiquidity. In worse times, people may regret not having liquidity.

- Flatt on Brookfield complexity: structure is complicated because they optimize for different investor needs, taxes, duration, and capital pools. He argues long-term investors have done well and Brookfield is transparent if people take time to understand it.

## Investing read

- Marks = cycle discipline: optimism high, credit loose, forward returns likely lower, keep dry powder / downside protection.

- Flatt = real-asset compounder: own hard-to-build infrastructure tied to power, data centers, and AI demand, but buy cash-flowing assets at sensible multiples.

- Best combined lesson: don’t chase AI IPOs blindly; look for bottleneck owners and disciplined capital allocators with balance-sheet strength.

## One-line summary

Marks says prepare for optimism to fade; Flatt says the AI/power/data-center buildout is real, but the money is in hard-to-build, cash-flowing assets owned with long-term discipline.
