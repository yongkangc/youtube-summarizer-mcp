# The Numbers are in: A Post-Prospectus SpaceX Valuation!

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NQKIJU7TmTc

Speaker: Aswath Damodaran

## TL;DR

Damodaran revisits his pre-prospectus SpaceX valuation after getting actual financials. The surprise is not the reported losses or cash burn; it is the sharper evidence that SpaceX is becoming a three-engine story: launch, Starlink/connectivity, and a much more aggressive xAI/enterprise-AI bet. His intrinsic value barely changes — roughly $1.25–$1.3T, or about $100/share — while reported IPO pricing around $135/share implies roughly $1.8T and looks too rich for him as an investor.

## Key points

- The prospectus is bloated but still useful. He frames it as part of modern “disclosure diarrhea”: hundreds of pages, many boilerplate sections, lots of distractions, but some important unit-economics and capital-intensity clues.

- Reported numbers mostly confirm his prior story:
  - Launch and Starlink revenues were close to his estimates.
  - xAI revenue was higher than expected, partly from leasing compute capacity.
  - He estimated a ~$2B operating loss; actual was about **$2.6B**.
  - Book equity came in around **$41.3B** versus his rough **$20B** placeholder.
  - Debt was **$22.9B**, but cash was almost **$25B**, so net debt is effectively negative.

- Share count/cash treatment matters, but not enough to move value much:
  - Pre-prospectus he guessed around **2.5B** shares; post-split disclosed count is about **12.5B**, excluding new IPO shares and employee restricted stock.
  - He expects actual diluted count above **13B**.
  - IPO proceeds are estimated around **$74.4–$75B**, and the company plans to keep the cash for operating/capex needs.

- Governance is clearly Musk-controlled:
  - Around **6.9B Class A** shares with one vote each.
  - Around **5.6B Class B** shares with ten votes each.
  - Musk effectively controls about **85%** of voting rights.
  - Damodaran already assumed this was an Elon vehicle; prospectus just quantifies it.

- Original valuation story had three operating buckets plus expansion options:
  - Launch: dominant player, **70%** share of a **$100B** market → **$70B** target revenue.
  - Starlink/connectivity: satellite broadband as a niche inside a **$1.6T** broadband/internet market → **$120B** target revenue.
  - xAI: originally assumed to stay more consumer-subscription focused → **$80B** target revenue and **45%** target margin.
  - Expansion options: Mars/space travel, broader broadband substitution, AI side businesses.

- Prospectus TAM claims are huge and should be discounted:
  - SpaceX prospectus claims total TAM around **$28T**.
  - Space launch TAM: **$370B** versus Damodaran’s **$100B** assumption.
  - Connectivity TAM: **$1.6T**, which he sees as the whole internet market, not satellite internet.
  - AI TAM: **$26T**, including **$22.7T** from enterprise solutions.
  - His view: obvious Silicon Valley TAM inflation, but it still signals xAI is going after a bigger enterprise market than he assumed.

- Growth mix is important:
  - Aggregate SpaceX revenue growth in 2025: about **33%**.
  - Launch grew only about **8%**.
  - Connectivity/Starlink grew about **49–50%** and is now the largest revenue slice.
  - xAI grew about **22%**.
  - So SpaceX is already less of a pure launch company and more a connectivity + AI company.

- Unit economics are mixed:
  - Launch gross margin improved from about **59.4%** to almost **67%**.
  - Connectivity gross margin improved from about **37%** to **48%**.
  - xAI gross margin worsened; it has worse unit economics and more competition.
  - Starlink ARPU fell from almost **$100/month** in 2024 to about **$66/month** in Q1 2026, but subscribers doubled, so total revenue still rose. He reads that as net positive if the goal is mass-market penetration.

- xAI is the biggest change and biggest risk:
  - Prospectus and the Colossus/compute-leasing story suggest xAI is going after enterprise services, not just consumer subscriptions.
  - Colossus leasing to Anthropic is cited as **$1.25B/month** of near-term revenue.
  - Damodaran doubles his xAI target revenue assumption, but cuts target margin hard because enterprise AI is more competitive and compute delivery costs are high.
  - xAI target operating margin falls from **45%** to **25%**.
  - More AI ambition means more capex and R&D; capex doubled in 2025 and R&D more than doubled, with AI driving much of the increase.

- Updated valuation inputs:
  - Launch margin raised from **40%** to **45%** due to improving unit economics.
  - Connectivity target margin stays at **60%**.
  - xAI target revenue is much higher, but target margin lower and reinvestment much heavier.
  - Higher Treasury yield pushes cost of capital from ~**8%** to **8.37%** for the next 10 years, then **8.25%** beyond.

- Updated value:
  - Operating asset value: about **$1.22T**.
  - Prior valuation: about **$1.21T**.
  - Including IPO cash and dilution, his equity value works out near **$100/share**.
  - Reported IPO price: about **$135/share**, implying roughly **$1.8T**.
  - His take: $135 looks rich versus intrinsic value.

- Best bearish case is not “it loses money”:
  - He calls that lazy. Young growth companies should look messy: losses, negative cash flow, low current revenues.
  - A real bearish case should attack target market size, future margins, capital intensity, competitive pressure, or governance.

- Bankers are mostly doing pricing theater:
  - He says bankers do not value companies; they price them by reference to prior private pricing and market mood.
  - SpaceX was already “pre-priced” in private markets around **$1.2–$1.25T**.
  - Bankers likely added a public-market premium and underpricing discount to get near **$1.8T**.
  - Marketing support is less valuable because Musk can sell the story with a tweet; aftermarket support is unrealistic for a multi-trillion-dollar stock.

- IPO trade vs investment:
  - As an investor, he would not buy at the IPO price.
  - He would not short either, because mood and momentum can dominate intrinsic value for a long time.
  - As a trader, the setup could still work if the stock opens at $1.8T and momentum takes it to $2T–$2.2T, but timing the exit is the whole game.
  - He compares it to Facebook and Uber: initially too expensive, later became attractive when price collapsed.

## Practical investor takeaway

If you are underwriting SpaceX, the right questions are not “is it currently profitable?” or “does the prospectus show losses?” The real underwriting variables are:

- How big is the true satellite internet market?
- Can Starlink keep scaling subs without destroying ARPU/margins?
- Does launch remain a high-margin quasi-monopoly?
- Is xAI’s enterprise-AI opportunity real, or a capex/R&D sinkhole?
- Will Musk’s control create upside from bold execution or downside from overreach?
- Is the entry price low enough to survive mood reversal after IPO hype?

## One-line summary

Damodaran’s post-prospectus SpaceX value barely changes, but the risk mix shifts: Starlink and launch look stronger, xAI looks bigger and more dangerous, and the rumored IPO price is too rich for an investor even if traders may chase it higher.
