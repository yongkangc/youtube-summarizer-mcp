# Sustainable Compounding Return Ladder

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Q3Jm22V-xq4

## TL;DR

Do not maximize headline return. Find highest return you can sustain for decades without ruin. For most people that means low-cost equity indexing near the `7%` real-return regime; higher targets require genuine information or operating edge, concentrated work, and much higher failure risk.

## Return ladder

### 3%: liquidity, not wealth creation

- Cash, CDs, and conservative bonds preserve optionality.
- After taxes and personal cost-of-living inflation, real return may be negative.
- Keep roughly `3–6 months` of expenses plus near-term purchases; avoid treating excess cash as long-term compounder.

### 7%: default wealth-building lane

- Approximate long-run real return cited for S&P 500.
- Use low-cost diversified index funds, contribute continuously, avoid timing.
- Video claims `$500/month` from age 25 to 65 becomes over `$1.2M`; starting at 35 produces roughly `$570k`.
- Edge is behavioral: low fees, tax efficiency, long horizon, no interruption.

### 14%: requires local edge

Potential sources:

- Deep knowledge of local property.
- Industry expertise developed over decades.
- Ability to identify and improve mismanaged small businesses.

This is active work, not passive alpha. If edge cannot be stated clearly, remain in 7% lane.

### 30%: owner-operator compounding

- Buy and improve small cash-generative businesses.
- Video argues a `3× EBITDA` acquisition starts near a `33%` pre-tax earnings yield.
- Reinvest into people, locations, systems, and operations.
- Boring local services—HVAC, plumbing, roofing—may support high returns at small scale.
- Returns compress as capital base grows and good reinvestment opportunities become scarce.

### 60%: early-stage hypergrowth

- Possible for small businesses during limited periods.
- Requires full-time obsession and operational skill.
- Rarely persists; survivorship bias and ruin risk are severe.
- Creator says Filterbuy once compounded above 60%, now targets about 30% at larger scale.

### 120%: venture power law

- Portfolio depends on one extreme winner covering many near-zero outcomes.
- Examples cited: `$25k` Coinbase seed investment becoming roughly `$10M`; Jason Calacanis's `$25k` Uber investment reportedly becoming over `$100M`.
- Access, selection, dilution, and total-loss risk make this unsuitable as a normal base plan.

## Theory of edge

Market pays higher returns only when investor supplies something scarce:

- patience and behavioral discipline at 7%;
- local information and work at 14%;
- operational execution and illiquidity at 30–60%;
- extreme uncertainty and power-law loss absorption at 120%.

No structural edge means high target return is leverage, concentration, luck, or hidden tail risk.

## Caveats

- `3× EBITDA = 33% return on capital` is oversimplified. Purchase price is not necessarily total invested capital; debt service, taxes, maintenance capex, working capital, customer concentration, and owner replacement costs reduce returns.
- High business returns are not liquid mark-to-market investment returns and often include unpaid operator labor.
- S&P return assumptions vary by start date, valuation, inflation, taxes, and fees.
- Extreme compounding projections are fragile: small return differences, drawdowns, and interruptions dominate long-horizon outcomes.

## YK read

Best message is not the target number. It is lane selection:

- Core liquid wealth: diversified 7% compounding.
- Seek 14–30% only where you possess auditable edge and control.
- Treat 60–120% as concentrated operating/venture exposure, not portfolio baseline.
- Optimize geometric return and survival, not arithmetic return fantasy.

## One-line summary

Highest sustainable return beats highest imaginable return; compounding only works if you survive long enough to keep it uninterrupted.
