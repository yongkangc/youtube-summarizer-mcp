# Warren Buffett on When Not to Sell a Stock

## TLDR

Buffett's sell rule depends on capital scarcity. Early in his career, ideas exceeded money, so he sold his least attractive holding whenever a more compelling opportunity appeared. Berkshire later had more money than ideas, so it generally held great businesses unless management disappointed badly or business economics changed materially—not merely because valuation looked high.

## Buffett's framework

### When capital is scarce

- Rank every holding against available alternatives.
- Sell weakest holding to fund clearly superior opportunity.
- Opportunity cost, not attachment or original purchase price, drives decision.
- Buffett avoided borrowing even when good ideas exceeded capital.

### When capital is abundant

Berkshire's inclination is permanent ownership because:

- replacement opportunities are scarce;
- high-quality businesses continue compounding;
- selling creates a new reinvestment problem;
- relationships with managers matter;
- wholly owned businesses joined Berkshire expecting permanent home.

Buffett said Berkshire would not sell See's Candies, Buffalo News or Borsheims even for three times his estimate of value. He acknowledged this might not maximize financial value, but it matches how he wants to operate and what he promises shareholders.

## Legitimate sell triggers

- Management becomes deeply discouraging.
- Business's economic characteristics change materially.
- Capital becomes chronically scarce while superior opportunities appear.
- Original thesis is no longer true.

High valuation alone is usually insufficient for Berkshire, although Buffett explicitly avoids making rule absolute.

## Structural edge

**Edge:** Permanent capital permits Buffett to hold durable compounders through volatility without satisfying short-term investors or constantly finding replacement trades. Patience avoids taxes, timing errors and reinvestment risk.

## Important caveat

This is not universal “never sell” advice. Berkshire was generating at least `$5B/year`—roughly `$100M/week`—in the 2001 example. Most investors face tighter capital, concentration and liquidity constraints. For them, extreme overvaluation can justify trimming when expected return falls below a better risk-adjusted alternative.

Buffett also mixes financial optimization with personal values: loyalty, relationships and being a permanent home. That is a chosen objective, not proof that refusing a three-times-value offer maximizes returns.

## Practical checklist

Before selling, ask:

1. Has management quality changed?
2. Have business economics or moat deteriorated?
3. Is thesis broken—or is price merely volatile/high?
4. Is there a clearly superior use of capital after tax?
5. Am I capital-constrained or inventing activity?
6. Does holding still fit concentration and risk limits?

## Bottom line

Sell because thesis, management or opportunity cost changed—not because price alone makes you uncomfortable. But apply Buffett's rule according to your own capital scarcity, risk limits and objectives.
