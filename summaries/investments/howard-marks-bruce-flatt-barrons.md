# Catching Up With Power Investors Howard Marks and Bruce Flatt | At Barron's

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZPpcUUe9--k

## TL;DR

Howard Marks and Bruce Flatt frame the current market as optimism-rich but not uniformly expensive. Marks says credit is generous after a long benign cycle, so investors should prepare for less optimistic times. Flatt says AI/data-center/electrification demand is real and underbuilt, but the right investing game is still long-duration ownership, operational value creation, and compounding.

## Key points

- Brookfield bought into Oaktree in 2019 because Brookfield had global infrastructure/equity scale but wanted best-in-class credit capability.
- The integration was intentionally slow: ~7 years to preserve Oaktree’s decentralized investment culture while adding Brookfield resources and capital.
- Oaktree keeps its brand. Marks says Brookfield’s capital-rich balance sheet improves alignment because Oaktree can put more capital into its own strategies alongside clients.
- Brookfield has about $1.3T of total assets and, according to Flatt, neither he nor Marks makes most individual decisions. Their role is more about culture, investment ethos, and when to press or slow down.
- Flatt says Brookfield’s stock has worked because the business has durable cash flows, a large balance sheet, insurance assets, infrastructure exposure, and equity capital invested beside clients.
- He cites Brookfield compounding at almost ~19% annualized over ~30 years.
- Marks’ equity-market view: optimism has been ascendant since around Oct 1, 2022, helped by the Fed turning more dovish. That optimism enables huge IPO valuations and market strength.
- Marks’ warning: in periods of optimism, part of you must always ask, “how do we prepare for less optimistic times?”
- Credit cycle: Marks says credit has been on a long tear since the GFC low in March 2009. The economy is strong, fear has receded, skepticism and due diligence have declined, and FOMO pushes lenders to cut price/standards.
- He does not see a reason to cut rates because the economy does not need stimulus and inflation should not be invited back.
- Flatt’s equity/private-market view: public tech/AI names are expensive, but many non-tech companies trade at low multiples. Brookfield has been taking companies private and sees good forward returns if it operates them well.
- On AI/electrification: Flatt sees a real productivity investment cycle across power, data centers, AI factories, offices, factories, and homes. If it works, it could drive one of the most productive periods in history.
- He pushes back on “bubble” framing for data centers/power. Expected demand is much larger than actual buildout because execution is hard: site power, connect to grid/plant, build facility, install chips, and fund projects of $20B–$250B.
- Brookfield is leasing to strong sovereign AI customers and top companies, some credit-rated better than countries.
- Marks on software/private-credit distress: no current spike in distress. There is a spike in worry, but many software companies are still servicing debt. He thinks broad worry may be excessive, though some companies will fail case by case.
- Brookfield as global/local investor: Flatt says Brookfield invests locally in each country — roads, water, power, data centers, compute — and focuses on countries with rule of law, economic tailwinds, and ability to earn returns.
- Brookfield has ~$600B of investments in the U.S., making it one of the largest private investors there.
- On Brookfield complexity: Flatt admits the structure takes time to understand but says it exists to optimize capital markets, taxes, duration, governance, and investor needs.
- Marks says private markets grew because leveraged investments worked well during falling rates and because the long positive environment made investors forget the value of liquidity.
- Final lesson from Flatt: investment success is about compounding wealth over very long periods by owning great businesses with great people who steward capital well.
- Final lesson from Marks: Buffett succeeded as an owner, not a trader. The next period will reward ownership mentality more than financial engineering.

## Investor read

- Marks is cautious on credit: easy borrowing + low fear + weak due diligence = poor forward underwriting environment. Not necessarily crisis tomorrow, but standards matter.
- Flatt is constructive on real assets and AI infrastructure: data-center/power demand is real, but supply is constrained by execution, capital, and permitting. That favors scaled operators.
- Important distinction: public AI/software valuations may be hot, while real-asset buildout and non-tech private-market opportunities can still be attractive.
- The shared framework is anti-trading: own assets, buy below value, use sensible structure, improve operations, and let compounding work.

## One-line summary

Marks says prepare for the other side of today’s optimism; Flatt says the AI infrastructure cycle is real but the winning approach remains ownership, execution, and long-term compounding.
