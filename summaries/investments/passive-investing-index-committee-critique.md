# Is passive investing really passive?

[YouTube source](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FNA6wHOufeQ)

## TL;DR

Jeff Joseph argues that “passive investing” is misleading branding: index funds delegate portfolio decisions to index-provider rules and committees rather than eliminating human judgment. His semantic critique is fair, but he stretches it into a broader indictment that does not overturn the main empirical case for broad, low-cost indexing.

## Core argument

- Every index requires rules for eligibility, weighting, additions, deletions, rebalancing, and exceptions.
- Committees can revise methodologies, causing investors to own different portfolios without directly voting on each change.
- Therefore index investing is not literally judgment-free; it is **rules-based delegated management**.
- Joseph says industry language has shifted in regulatory filings from “passive” toward “index tracking” or “rules-based,” while marketing still uses passive framing.

## Examples and numbers

- Speaker claims index funds manage roughly **$21T** in American savings.
- Nasdaq and FTSE Russell allegedly changed eligibility rules to accommodate non-exchange-listed SpaceX, while S&P 500 declined inclusion. He uses this to show different providers can reach different portfolio decisions.
- S&P reportedly revised methodology eight times between 2015 and 2018.
- Tesla's December 2020 S&P 500 inclusion allegedly triggered roughly **$80B** of mechanical buying and moved price about **4%**.
- He cites research finding S&P 500 additions historically gained roughly **3%** around inclusion, benefiting anticipatory traders at index-fund expense.
- Nasdaq 100 ran a special 2023 rebalance to reduce Mag 7 concentration.
- World allegedly has **3M+ indices** versus about **60K listed stocks**, showing many indices are manufactured investment theses rather than neutral market maps.
- Passive funds reportedly hold nearly **55%** of US equity AUM, versus about 20% in 2010.
- BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street collectively own roughly **20–25%** of many S&P 500 companies.

These figures are presented by speaker; video does not provide visible source links or enough methodological context to audit every number.

## Steelman acknowledged by speaker

Joseph concedes strongest pro-index evidence:

- low fees matter;
- most large-cap active managers underperform benchmarks over long periods after fees;
- SPIVA results replicate across periods and regions.

His narrower point: active-manager underperformance does not prove index construction lacks discretion, nor that every investor is incapable of beating a benchmark.

## Where argument is strongest

- “Passive” describes implementation relative to a benchmark, not absence of upstream judgment.
- Custom thematic/factor/ESG indices often function like systematic active strategies with index branding.
- Predictable rebalances can create execution costs and temporary price pressure.
- Index-provider governance, methodology drift, and concentration deserve due diligence.
- Benchmark-relative accountability can obscure whether benchmark itself remains sensible.

## Where argument overreaches

- Human-authored rules do not make broad-market indexing equivalent to discretionary stock picking. Rules are transparent, repeatable, and generally low-turnover.
- Front-running/reconstitution costs are real but must be compared with active-management fees, taxes, turnover, and selection errors.
- Index funds can lag their benchmark through fees and tracking error; they are not literally incapable of underperformance.
- Common ownership causing weaker competition is contested and does not establish that index investors earn inferior returns.
- Concentrated voting power at asset managers is governance concern, separate from whether low-cost index exposure is sound portfolio construction.
- Proving some investors can beat market does not identify them ex ante or show self-directed retail trading has better expected outcomes.

## Edge and investor read-through

**Potential edge:** Predictable index additions, deletions, and special rebalances may create temporary forced-flow dislocations. Market pays traders who forecast constituent changes and supply liquidity before/after mechanical demand.

**Risks to that edge:** crowded anticipation, uncertain committee timing, borrow/option costs, reversals after inclusion, and declining inclusion premium as more capital arbitrages it.

For investors:

- Prefer broad, low-cost, transparent, low-turnover indices.
- Read methodology, concentration limits, rebalance rules, and committee discretion.
- Treat thematic/factor indices as active strategies; demand explicit theory of edge.
- Separate benchmark-selection decision from fund-implementation decision.
- Do not abandon indexing merely because “passive” is imperfect terminology.

## YK read

Best takeaway is governance, not conspiracy: **you always choose a portfolio manager somewhere—person, committee, or ruleset.** Audit that ruleset. Broad indexing remains excellent default for exposure without specific edge; systematic active or self-directed bets need a structural reason market should pay you.

## One-line summary

Passive funds remove discretionary trading at fund level, not judgment from index design.
