# Warren Buffett on Berkshire's Alphabet Investment

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vaHbb3-tHQA

## TL;DR

Buffett says he initiated Berkshire’s Alphabet position, while Greg Abel approved it and remains final decision-maker. His thesis is not “AI excitement”; it is Alphabet’s probability of sustaining returns on capital well above Treasuries while reinvesting enormous amounts of cash productively.

## Position

- Video says Berkshire holds more than `$31B` of Alphabet.
- Position began in `Q3 2025` and expanded after Greg Abel became CEO.
- Roughly `$10B` was reportedly added through a private stock purchase the prior month.
- Buffett says he initiated the idea, but he and Abel discuss decisions daily and Abel is now final decider.
- Among publicly traded holdings, Alphabet reportedly trails only Apple and American Express; Buffett evaluates wholly owned businesses such as BNSF on same economic basis.

## Buffett's investment framework

- Buy a good business on good terms with strong management.
- Good business earns returns on capital materially above essentially risk-free Treasury returns.
- Best business can reinvest excess capital internally at similarly high rates.
- Duration matters: sustained high ROIC creates compounding that becomes enormous over decades.
- Example: American Express earns `30%+` on capital versus many banks around `13–14%`, without proportionately greater risk.
- Real cash generation matters more than whether company appears technologically exciting.

## Why Alphabet now

- Buffett admits avoiding asset-light technology earlier was a mistake.
- Alphabet and other hyperscalers now deploy hundreds of billions into AI infrastructure.
- Market dislikes rising capex, but Buffett thinks Alphabet’s record makes it more likely than most companies to earn attractive returns on that spending.
- Core underwriting question: incremental internal rate of return on AI capex versus Treasuries—not next quarter's headline growth.
- Alphabet appears preferred because Buffett trusts its ability to convert investment into durable customer value and high returns, though he says four or five Berkshire businesses remain better.

## Competitive warning

- Hyperscalers may be forced into an expensive game they would prefer not to play.
- Historical leaders can lose apparently dominant positions when competitors serve customers better; IBM and A&P are examples.
- Customer value, not Wall Street narrative, determines durability.

## Theory of edge

Berkshire’s edge is patience and scale: underwrite long-duration incremental ROIC while market fixates on quarterly capex and AI winners. Market pays if Alphabet’s current spending depresses near-term free cash flow but builds durable returns above its cost of capital.

## Kill tests

- AI capex earns returns near or below Treasuries/cost of capital.
- Search economics structurally weaken faster than AI monetization grows.
- Competitive spending becomes mandatory but non-differentiating.
- Alphabet cannot convert infrastructure scale into customer retention and cash flow.

## One-line summary

Buffett bought Alphabet because he sees a proven high-ROIC compounder capable of reinvesting AI-scale capital—not because he suddenly became a technology forecaster.
