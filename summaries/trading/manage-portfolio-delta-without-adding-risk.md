# Manage Your Portfolio Delta (without adding risk) in 3 Steps | Options Strategies 2023

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yZFLbgcVK3Y

## TL;DR

Tastytrade’s point: after a large market move, you can adjust portfolio delta by **removing existing risk** instead of adding new contrarian trades. Into a big rally, close bullish winners/scratches to reduce positive delta; into a big selloff, close bearish winners/scratches to reduce negative delta. You move delta closer to neutral while reducing total exposure.

## Core idea

Most traders think “I’m too bullish after a rally, so I need to add bearish trades.”

Their preferred framing:

- Don’t add new risk first.
- Look for existing positions that already benefited from the move.
- Close winners or scratches that carry delta in the same direction as the move.
- This naturally brings portfolio delta closer to zero.
- You reduce risk and synthetically lean contrarian at the same time.

## Market context

They discuss a very volatile period with large E-mini / SPX moves:

- Multiple days of **+100 / -100 point** whiplash.
- VIX around **32–33**, implying roughly **2% daily expected moves**.
- The day’s E-mini move was around **2.5%** — big, slightly outside expected, but not insane relative to VIX in the 30s.
- With VIX still around **30**, a full reversal the next day would still be within implied expectations.

## Example: rally day portfolio adjustment

On a big rally, he looks for bullish positions to close:

- Closed one **Amazon call diagonal spread**.
- Closed three **short puts**.
- All four were bullish positions.

Effect:

- Before closing, assume portfolio had large positive delta, e.g. **+180 deltas**.
- Closing bullish positions removes positive delta.
- Portfolio delta moves closer to zero.
- Total risk also drops because positions are removed.

This is different from adding new bearish trades, which would reduce net delta but increase gross risk.

## Why closing positions changes delta contrarianly

Closing a bullish position into a rally is synthetically bearish / risk-reducing:

- Closing a long call diagonal means selling out of call exposure.
- Closing short puts means buying back puts after the stock/market has rallied.
- Those closing transactions add opposite delta versus the move.
- But because they close positions, they reduce open risk rather than add new exposure.

So into a rally:

- Remove bullish winners/scratches.
- Book gains or reduce risk.
- Lower portfolio delta.
- Improve position if market reverses tomorrow.

Into a selloff, reverse the logic:

- Remove bearish winners/scratches.
- Reduce negative delta.
- Bring portfolio back toward neutral without adding new bullish risk.

## Why this works well in choppy markets

They are not claiming every rally must reverse. The point is that in high-volatility regimes, big moves are often followed by large counter-moves, and implied volatility already says those moves are plausible.

Examples from SPX over the prior year:

- January: SPX fell from around **4600 to 4200**, then rebounded to **4400** within five trading days.
- March: SPX moved from around **4100 to 4500**, then back toward **4200** weeks later.
- Many examples of hundreds-of-points reversals.

In these regimes, removing same-direction delta after a large move can accidentally “paint tops and bottoms,” but the important part is not prediction — it is reducing risk while improving delta posture.

## Trading read-through

This is a clean portfolio-management rule: adjust delta first by **closing risk**, not adding risk.

For a short-premium / options book, gross exposure matters. If you add bearish trades after a rally, you may neutralize delta but increase total positions, margin usage, correlation, and complexity. If instead you close bullish winners/scratches, you reduce delta and gross risk simultaneously.

That is much cleaner process:

- No need to forecast reversal with high conviction.
- No need to add a fresh low-edge trade into a whippy market.
- You harvest existing profits and reduce portfolio sensitivity.
- If reversal happens, you are better off than if you left everything on.
- If rally continues, you gave up some remaining upside, but with less risk.

## Practical 3-step version

1. **Measure the move vs implied vol**
   - Is today’s move large relative to VIX / expected move?
   - If VIX is 30+, a 2% reversal is not crazy.

2. **Find positions aligned with the move**
   - Big rally: find bullish winners/scratches — short puts, call diagonals, long delta spreads.
   - Big selloff: find bearish winners/scratches — short calls, put diagonals, negative delta spreads.

3. **Close risk before adding risk**
   - Take off those positions first.
   - Reduce gross exposure.
   - Bring portfolio delta closer to neutral.
   - Only add new contrarian trades if delta/risk still requires it.

## One-line summary

After a big move, don’t add contrarian risk first — close same-direction winners to reduce delta and gross exposure at the same time.
