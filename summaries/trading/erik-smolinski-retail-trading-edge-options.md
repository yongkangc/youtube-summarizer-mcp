# Erik Smolinski: Retail Edge, Market Effects, and Options Execution

[Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fVTepbK7fK8)

## TL;DR

Smolinski's central framework is correct: **options are instruments, not edge**. Start with a persistent market effect, explain why market pays for bearing or exploiting it, prove it survives friction, then select derivative structure matching expected path, volatility, and risk.

His claimed performance—nearly 100% in 2025 and no losing year since 2008—is presented without audited returns, drawdowns, leverage, capital base, or benchmark. Treat process insights seriously; do not infer reproducible returns.

## Theory of edge

> Retail gets paid where structural risk premia, behavioral overreaction, institutional constraints, or slow large-player flows create persistent effects that remain monetizable after spreads, slippage, fees, borrow, and taxes.

Examples discussed:

- variance risk premium;
- momentum and sector rotation;
- leader/laggard continuation;
- institutional accumulation/distribution footprints;
- month-end window dressing;
- neglected illiquid markets where large institutions cannot participate efficiently.

Finding statistical effect is only first step. It becomes trading edge only after defining **profit mechanism**, implementation, capacity, and failure regime.

## 2025 performance explanation

Smolinski attributes exceptional recent years to:

- volatile thematic markets offering large derivative moves;
- trading observable direction instead of fighting valuation;
- rotating capital among profit mechanisms according to regime;
- not targeting fixed annual return.

Example: Palantir could remain long despite extreme P/E if price/flow/theme continued upward. Market being expensive is not entry signal by itself.

He says current news-heavy regime weakened variance-risk-premium trades because realized movement repeatedly caught up with implied volatility. Edge allocation must change with regime.

## Sector rotation and trade expression

Workflow:

1. Find sector exhibiting relative strength or emerging breakout.
2. Rank leaders/laggards cross-sectionally and through time.
3. Prefer leaders consolidating while holding relative strength.
4. Enter as sector breakout releases previously suppressed leader.
5. Choose instrument from option-chain economics—not arbitrary price target.

Structures mentioned:

- synthetic longs;
- long calls with longer duration;
- ratio call diagonals.

Typical ratio diagonal:

- long call at least ~90 DTE;
- long strike around 50–80 delta depending desired convexity;
- sell shorter calls roughly 2–4 weeks out at ratio designed to subsidize carry;
- preserve uncapped upside by keeping net long-call exposure.

This structure is not automatically safe. Risks include term-structure shifts, front-leg assignment, gap-through-short strikes, changing delta ratio, wide markets, and path dependence.

## Process for new traders

### 1. Stop optimizing starting-account P&L

Build repeatable process first. Save capital and hold broad buy-and-hold exposure while learning rather than forcing trades to make small account grow quickly.

### 2. Write plan yourself

Document:

- market effect;
- causal profit mechanism;
- entry/exit;
- sizing;
- expected path and timing;
- volatility assumptions;
- friction;
- failure states;
- postmortem fields.

Writing exposes unanswered questions before capital does.

### 3. Learn options deeply

Correct directional view can still lose because trader overpaid implied volatility, selected wrong duration/strike, or ignored volatility collapse. Derivative must fit thesis; do not force familiar vertical onto every idea.

### 4. Paper trade seriously

Practice execution and tracking before live capital. Smolinski used parallel paper accounts at actual size and 10× size to normalize larger P&L numbers.

### 5. Judge process over sample

After every trade ask why outcome occurred, whether process was followed, and whether mechanism remains alive. One win does not validate edge; one loss does not invalidate it.

### 6. Train temperament

Lean into strengths, constrain weaknesses. Risk-averse traders need controlled exposure; risk-open traders need sizing and pre-trade constraints. Smolinski suggests dampening emotional reaction to wins first, reducing symmetric pain from losses.

## Genuine retail advantages

- **Agility:** small capital moves without block-trade or mandate constraints.
- **Optionality not to trade:** no requirement to stay invested, quote markets, or use every strategy.
- **Capacity:** can operate in opportunities too small or illiquid for institutions.
- **Flow riding:** can follow gradual institutional accumulation/distribution.

His claim that illiquidity can contain edge needs strong caveat: apparent alpha may be stale marks or uncollectable spread. Exit liquidity, fill assumptions, adverse selection, and mark methodology decide whether edge is real.

## Research pathway

Smolinski recommends:

- SSRN for market-effect research;
- *Quantitative Momentum* by Wesley Gray as beginner synthesis;
- replicate known results before inventing strategies;
- record structured observations suitable for data analysis;
- use out-of-sample, walk-forward, historical-regime, Monte Carlo, and synthetic stress tests.

Beginner should master one documented effect rather than half-build six unrelated strategies. Expand toolbox after replication process works.

## What interview gets right

- Every trade must answer why market pays.
- Instrument selection follows edge, not reverse.
- Friction separates anomaly from monetizable strategy.
- Regime dependence matters.
- Retail's best advantages are small size and freedom, not superior information.
- Process and risk survival precede return maximization.

## Missing evidence / risks

- Claimed return history unaudited.
- No annual return series, drawdowns, Sharpe, leverage, beta, tail exposure, or capacity.
- Nearly 100% return can reflect exceptional edge, concentrated factor exposure, convexity, leverage, or lucky regime.
- Ratio diagonals and illiquid options require executable fill assumptions and assignment/gap stress.
- VWAP footprints/window dressing are known effects; crowding and transaction costs can erase them.
- “No losing year” says little about intra-year drawdown or hidden short-volatility exposure.

## YK read

Best takeaway is workflow:

`structural reason → measurable effect → executable profit mechanism → option structure → friction/stress test → sizing → postmortem`

Do not copy his return target or structures blindly. Replicate one effect with real quotes and conservative fills; prove robustness across regimes; then choose derivatives. His strongest lesson is not a trade—it is refusing to confuse options complexity with edge.
