# 3 Ways to Trade a Semiconductor Rebound Without Calling the Bottom

[Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6XjRPf5FZ2M)

## TL;DR

The hosts compare bullish semiconductor rebound structures: call diagonal, ZEBRA, and broken-wing butterfly. Choice depends on **volatility level, desired delta, time horizon, and whether view is broad rebound or specific-price bounce**.

For current SMH conditions in the clip—down >11% from June high, still up ~83% YTD, IVR ~89—they prefer keeping it simple with a defined-risk put spread rather than forcing stock ownership or complex ratio risk.

## Theory of edge

> If semiconductor implied volatility is elevated after a selloff, trader may get paid to warehouse rebound/mean-reversion risk by selling rich downside premium, while avoiding exact bottom-calling through defined-risk structures.

Edge is not “semis fell.” Edge requires premium rich enough versus downside/gap risk, liquidity, and correlation to existing AI/semiconductor exposure.

## Strategy selection

### Call diagonal

- Buy back-month call.
- Sell front-month call.
- Best when IV is relatively low and trader wants long vega.
- More useful when thesis needs time.

Good for: “I want bullish exposure, but rebound timing uncertain.”

Risks: front-month short call caps/complicates upside, term-structure shifts, long-vega exposure if IV falls.

### ZEBRA

- Typically buy two ITM calls, sell one OTM call.
- More delta/stock-replacement trade than vega trade.
- Most aggressively bullish of the three.

Good for: “I want stock-like upside with less capital than shares.”

Risks: still directional; can lose if underlying keeps falling; strike selection matters.

### Broken-wing butterfly

- Used for specific targeted bounce zone.
- Works better when IV is elevated.
- Hosts describe an unbalanced butterfly with embedded short vertical.

Good for: “I expect bounce toward this area, not unlimited upside.”

Risks: path and pin-zone dependent; payoff can look cleaner than actual fills/management.

## SMH example

SMH around 572 in clip. Selling naked puts possible but capital-heavy:

- example August 510 put uses roughly $5,000 buying power;
- expensive underlying makes naked puts less attractive for smaller accounts;
- IVR ~89 and IV ~60% make defined-risk premium sale more attractive.

Preferred simple trade:

- sell put spread around 535/540 zone;
- target roughly one-third of spread width as credit;
- check open interest/liquidity before entering/exiting.

This is not a bottom call. It is selling downside premium with capped loss.

## Pre-market options pricing

They do not trust IV or multi-leg pricing much pre-market unless symbol is extremely liquid and spreads match regular-hours norms.

Reason:

- IV calculations use option mid-price;
- wider bid/ask makes mid unstable;
- quotes may be real, but quote quality weak;
- illiquid tickers worse.

Rule: build mental database of normal regular-session markets. If pre-market spread is wider than normal, wait.

## Put ratio spread vs put spread

Question: when downside skew is expensive, does put ratio spread beat regular put spread?

Answer: regular put spread is cleaner. Put ratio spread adds extra naked short put.

1x2 put ratio spread =

- long one higher-strike put;
- short two lower-strike puts;
- equivalent to long put vertical plus naked short lower-strike put.

This can look high-probability and credit-friendly, but if market crashes:

- long vertical max value may be delayed by high IV before expiry;
- extra naked put drives losses;
- tail can overwhelm credit.

Use only if strongly bullish, account can handle margin/loss, and trader accepts crash-risk sale. Not interchangeable with defined-risk vertical.

## 21 DTE management

For defined-risk short premium that is only up 20–30% at 21 DTE:

- Tasty rule: manage around 21 DTE rather than wait for 50% profit target.
- Take smaller profit or roll to later cycle if thesis remains.
- Verticals decay slowly; waiting for 50% may require holding near expiry.
- Gamma risk rises into expiration.

For new traders, follow rule strictly: close/roll/manage at 21 DTE. Experienced traders can bend rules only intentionally.

## YK read

Best practical answer:

- Low IV + time needed → call diagonal.
- Need stock-like bullish delta → ZEBRA.
- Elevated IV + target bounce → broken-wing butterfly.
- High IVR semis with expensive shares → defined-risk put spread may be simplest.

Big warning: put ratio spread is not “better put spread.” It is a put spread plus hidden naked crash-risk leg. Use only when willing to sell downside tail.

For SMH/semis, check portfolio-level AI beta first. Short put spread on SMH may look defined risk at trade level but still adds to same AI/semiconductor factor already in book.
