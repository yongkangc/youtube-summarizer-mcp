# SpaceX IPO a Buy? Here are Better Alternatives!

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hky-Ag3F2Zc

## TL;DR

The speaker argues SpaceX is a phenomenal business but likely a poor risk/reward at the rumored IPO valuation of $1.75–2.0T. His preferred way to get space-economy exposure is via Amazon and Alphabet, where Kuiper, SpaceX stake, and other space/AI assets may be underappreciated relative to the core business valuation.

## Key points

- SpaceX IPO structure:
  - Rumored valuation: $1.75–2.0T.
  - Shares offered: 4.28% of company.
  - Total raise/sale: about $75B.
  - Primary shares: about $50B goes to SpaceX.
  - Vendor/insider shares: about $25B goes to existing holders selling out.
  - Retail allocation: about $22.5B, roughly 3x normal major IPO retail allocation.
  - Speaker flags this as a warning: high retail allocation may mean institutions are less willing to absorb the deal at that price.

- SpaceX business quality:
  - Speaker thinks SpaceX is a “phenomenal business” with a wide moat.
  - Four main businesses:
    - Starlink: satellite internet, recurring high-margin revenue, over 9M users.
    - Launch services: NASA, Department of Defense, and commercial launches.
    - Starshield: secure satellite network for defense/national security.
    - Starship: reusable heavy-lift rocket system for deep space / huge payloads.
  - 2025 numbers quoted:
    - Revenue: $15–16B.
    - EBITDA: about $8B.
    - Starlink: about 60% of revenue.
    - Launch services: about 80% of commercial mass-to-orbit.
  - 2030 projection quoted:
    - Revenue: $50B.
    - EBITDA: $25B.
    - Implied EBITDA CAGR: about 25.6%.

- Valuation problem:
  - At $1.75T valuation and 2025 revenue of $16B:
    - Price/sales: about 109x.
    - Price/EBITDA: about 218x.
  - Even using aggressive one-year doubling assumptions:
    - Revenue: $32B.
    - EBITDA: $16B.
    - Forward price/sales: about 54x.
    - Forward price/EBITDA: about 109x.
  - His core framing: great business ≠ great investment if the entry price is absurd.

- Comparison versus Amazon and Alphabet:
  - Amazon around $2T market cap:
    - Revenue: about $716B.
    - EBITDA: about $146B.
    - Price/sales: about 3x.
    - Price/EBITDA: about 16x.
  - Alphabet around $3.8T market cap:
    - Revenue: about $400B.
    - EBITDA: about $150B.
    - Price/sales: about 9.5x.
    - Price/EBITDA: about 25.5x.
  - Speaker asks: if you had $2T, would you rather own SpaceX with $8B EBITDA, Amazon with $146B EBITDA, or roughly half of Alphabet with $150B EBITDA?
  - His answer: Amazon/Alphabet offer better risk/reward — “a bird in the hand is worth more than two in the bush.”

- Alternative space-economy exposure:
  - Amazon:
    - Amazon Kuiper directly competes with Starlink.
    - Deploying over 3,200 low-earth-orbit satellites.
    - Advantages cited:
      - AWS integration: connectivity + cloud + security + device management bundle.
      - Financial firepower: about $70B annual operating cash flow; no need for dilutive fundraising.
      - Second-source demand: governments/enterprises want a credible alternative to Starlink.
    - Speaker’s view: Kuiper is effectively a free call option embedded in Amazon because current valuation mostly prices core businesses.
  - Alphabet:
    - Owns about 7% of SpaceX, initially invested $900M in 2015.
    - Also owns stakes in Anthropic, Stripe, Waymo, AST SpaceMobile, Planet Labs.
    - Speaker frames Alphabet as “Berkshire Hathaway of tech” — public core business plus underappreciated private venture portfolio.

- IPO warning:
  - IPO issuer wants the highest price possible; insiders selling want exit liquidity.
  - Speaker’s joke: IPO = “It’s Probably Overpriced” / “Insiders Profit Only.”
  - But he also admits hype can push overpriced IPOs higher short-term because short-term price is driven by emotion, not fundamentals.

## Speculative IPO trading framework

If someone wants to trade the IPO as momentum rather than invest on fundamentals:

- Only consider if the company is genuinely good; avoid low-quality IPOs.
- Wait for initial IPO pop and mark the first major high.
- Entry 1: buy after a 40–50% drop from the initial pop high.
- Entry 2: buy when price closes back above the initial pop high.
- Positioning: either split between E1 and E2, or choose one.
- Exit for speculative trade:
  - Sell half when daily price closes below 20 EMA.
  - Sell remaining half when 20 EMA crosses below 40 EMA.
- Examples used:
  - CoreWeave: initial pop, 40–50% drawdown, then rebound.
  - Reddit: similar post-IPO reset, later close above initial high.
  - Facebook/Meta: fell ~61% from IPO high, but became a huge long-term compounder.

## My read

Useful lens: this is not anti-SpaceX. It is anti-paying venture-growth fantasy multiples after insiders already choose the exit price. The cleanest investment point is: if you want space upside, Amazon/Alphabet may offer cheaper embedded optionality with real current earnings, while SpaceX IPO buyers may be underwriting the most optimistic future upfront.

## One-line summary

SpaceX may be an extraordinary company, but at a $1.75–2T IPO valuation the speaker thinks Amazon and Alphabet offer cheaper, better-underwritten space-economy exposure.
