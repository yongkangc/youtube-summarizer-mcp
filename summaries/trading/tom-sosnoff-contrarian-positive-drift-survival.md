# Tom Sosnoff: Contrarian Trading, Positive Drift and Survival

## TLDR

Tom Sosnoff reconciles two apparently conflicting beliefs: markets rise over time, but active traders often profit by taking the other side of urgent short-term flow. His process is not a permanent bearish forecast. It is repeated liquidity provision and option-premium selling, with position sizing and delta management designed so one extreme move hurts but does not end the game.

The interview is promotional for Lossdog and provides no audited trading record, exact strategy rules or risk-adjusted benchmark.

## Contrarianism versus positive drift

Sosnoff became contrarian as an open-outcry market maker: receiving a trade required accepting the opposite side of what another participant wanted immediately.

He defines positive drift broadly as improvement in markets, technology, careers and society. At market level, he cites roughly:

- **53/47** historical odds of up versus down periods;
- closer to **60/40** over the previous approximately two decades.

These figures are speaker claims; timeframe and sampling unit are not specified.

The reconciliation:

- long-term portfolio and civilization bias can remain positive;
- short-term trading can fade urgency, crowding and overpriced optionality;
- option premium can be sold without holding a permanent market-direction view;
- short delta can hedge positive delta from short puts or broader long exposure rather than express outright bearishness.

## Theory of edge

**Liquidity provision / risk premium:** traders demanding immediacy or convex protection may overpay. Premium sellers earn compensation for warehousing inventory, volatility and tail risk.

Edge requires more than frequent activity:

- implied volatility must exceed subsequently realized volatility after costs;
- spreads and fills must be executable;
- sizing must survive gaps and volatility expansion;
- exposures must not secretly collapse into one correlated short-volatility bet;
- delta hedges must not consume the entire premium.

Positive drift helps long portfolios but does not itself prove short-option expected value.

## Why options appeal to him

Options separate direction from payoff construction:

- one may be directionally wrong but profit through time decay or volatility contraction;
- one may be directionally right but lose because option price, timing or volatility was wrong;
- structure can change probability, capital use and path dependence.

Sosnoff views this strategic flexibility as more important than market forecasts. He says individual product choice matters little if the instrument is:

- liquid;
- fungible;
- exchange-traded;
- centrally cleared.

## Survival rule

He accepts that short premium occasionally suffers severe losses. His rule:

> A large move may hurt, but no single move should wipe out the portfolio.

He attributes most trader failures to:

1. believing markets are unfair and entering with no confidence in process;
2. weak mechanical understanding;
3. trading too large;
4. concentrating excessively in one thesis.

He says his losing years came from getting fixated on a particular view—evidence that directional conviction can override otherwise mechanical risk controls.

## Activity and ideas

Sosnoff reportedly makes more than **10,000 trades annually**. He treats trading as a decision-making laboratory and game rather than relying on individual trades to change his wealth.

Lossdog plans to expose his trade feed to generate questions and ideas—for example, why he might trade Treasury notes, sell crude-oil puts or sell out-of-the-money SpaceX puts.

Copying a feed is not a strategy. Followers would still need:

- entry rationale;
- implied-volatility context;
- portfolio-level delta and correlation;
- buying-power use;
- adjustment and exit rules;
- full loss history.

## Performance caveat

Sosnoff says he has:

- made and lost millions across trades;
- experienced losing trading years;
- never had a losing year across his total investment portfolio;
- never been wiped out;
- built businesses that never had a losing month.

He declines to provide trading P&L or benchmark comparison. Business exits and total-portfolio gains cannot validate active-trading alpha. Ten thousand trades per year also prove activity, not profitability.

His claim that roughly half of ordinary traders can succeed and around one-sixth can become highly successful is unsupported in the interview.

## YK read

Useful principles:

- Do not confuse contrarian execution with permanent bearishness.
- Express uncertainty through structure and sizing rather than prediction confidence.
- Cap correlated short-volatility and crash exposure at portfolio level.
- Judge every trade against executable expected value, not engagement or entertainment.
- Separate active-trading P&L from business wealth, passive beta and private investments.
- Repetition compounds judgment only when decisions, fills, Greeks and outcomes are logged and reviewed.

Sosnoff's personal scale makes small trades psychologically easy. A smaller trader cannot infer appropriate sizing by copying his contract count.

## Bottom line

Sosnoff's durable lesson is survival: take the other side of expensive urgency, use options to shape payoffs, and size so one adverse move cannot terminate compounding. His public record in this interview does not establish that frequent premium selling beats passive markets after tail losses and costs.
