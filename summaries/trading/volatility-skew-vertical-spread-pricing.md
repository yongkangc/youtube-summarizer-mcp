# Volatility Skew and Vertical-Spread Pricing

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SZ-1WiRt3kQ

## TL;DR

Equal-distance OTM call and put spreads can have very different credits because implied volatility varies by strike. Before selling a vertical, inspect whether the short leg has higher or lower IV than the long leg: ideally sell higher IV and buy lower IV.

## Mechanics

- Indexes commonly show downside skew: farther OTM puts carry higher IV because investors pay for crash protection.
- High-growth single stocks may show upside call skew from demand for explosive-upside exposure.
- Some names show flat/two-sided skew, pricing large moves both ways.
- Skew indicates where options demand prices tail risk; it does not predict direction.

## Examples

- SPY near 741–742:
  - Short `731/726` put spread, $5 wide: about `$1.34` credit.
  - Schultz prefers roughly one-third width, about `$1.67–1.70`.
  - Short `752/757` call spread, same approximate distance/width: nearly `$1` richer because skew made selling the short call IV versus buying wing IV more favorable.
- Tesla near 405:
  - `390/385` put spread and `420/425` call spread both around `$1.90`, suggesting flatter/two-sided skew.
- He warns premarket IV can be stale. An isolated `15.37` IV surrounded by roughly `13.9–14.4` likely reflects stale quote, not genuine skew.

## Theory of edge

Vertical seller earns short-vol/tail-risk premium. Skew identifies where protection/speculation demand is richest, but richer credit usually compensates for more adverse tail risk—not free mispricing.

## Checklist

- Match spread width, expiry, and approximate OTM distance/delta.
- Compare IV of short leg versus long wing.
- Prefer `sell higher IV / buy lower IV` when thesis and risk fit.
- Check live markets; ignore stale premarket anomalies.
- Evaluate credit/width and max loss, not credit alone.

## One-line summary

Spread direction alone does not determine price; volatility skew determines whether wing construction helps or taxes you.
