# Tom Sosnoff on trading, risk, and entrepreneurship

[YouTube source](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1etuJnbkGMM)

## TL;DR

Tom Sosnoff's core lesson: take lots of **probabilistic, liquid, survivable risk**, decide quickly, and keep building. His career moved from CBOE market making to founding Thinkorswim, tastytrade, and Lost Dog, with trading shaping his speed, comfort with uncertainty, and obsession with staying active.

His rhetoric sometimes blurs calculated risk with reckless concentration. The durable lesson is not “go all in”; it is to take risk where odds, liquidity, cash generation, and personal time horizon let you survive failure.

## Key points

- **Original trading edge:** As a CBOE market maker, Sosnoff earned spread by buying bids and selling offers against customer order flow. Structural edge was privileged floor access, speed, and liquidity provision, not directional forecasting.
- **Decision speed:** He argues trading rewires the brain to process uncertainty fast. Edge comes from quicker decisions and comfort with probabilistic outcomes, not secret intelligence.
- **Thinkorswim bet:** In 1999, he and Scott Sheridan sold their exchange seats and put prior decade's earnings into Thinkorswim. Company was profitable from first month. It later raised $22.5M at $100M valuation but reportedly never needed capital.
- **Risk control beneath bravado:** Free cash flow mattered because Nasdaq fell about 80% and external financing could disappear. His successful “risk-taking” paired concentration with a cash-generative business.
- **1987 lesson:** Before modern volatility skew, calls and puts were priced more symmetrically and markets underpriced crash velocity. Bigger fear was clearing/broker solvency, not only mark-to-market loss. Counterparty and market-structure risk can overwhelm modeled position risk.
- **Current systemic concern:** He names complacency plus leverage as dangerous, while admitting specific crisis catalyst cannot be predicted. Post-2008 liquidity intervention reduces some default risk but may deepen complacency.
- **Quant content strategy:** After selling Thinkorswim, tastytrade targeted gap between fundamental opinion and technical-chart content. It focused on expected move, options math, and research, wrapped in entertaining live programming. Technology and fees commoditize; content and attention differentiate.
- **Second company:** TD reportedly offered $200M for tastytrade's network after five years. Team declined, built brokerage, then sold business four years after launch for $1.1B. Sosnoff stayed to help acquirer and “honor the trade.”
- **Personal portfolio:** Still manually makes many futures, stocks, options, and futures-options trades. Private investing centers on founders and strategic learning in areas such as AI, data science, crypto, and quantum. These are personal practices, not a reproducible allocation framework.
- **Real-estate view:** He dislikes illiquidity, opaque pricing, fees, and leverage, and owns little beyond residences/offices. He frames much of past property appreciation as a multi-decade falling-rate trade unlikely to repeat identically.
- **Work philosophy:** Rejects retirement; building is source of meaning and legacy. Advises young founders to attempt more, accept failures, and avoid optimizing entire lives around preserving current capital.
- **Reputation compounds:** Staying after exits, improving buyer outcomes, and leaving counterparties better off creates future opportunity. Interview closes with “honoring your trade” and being long-term greedy.

## Edge and risk

**Theory of edge:** Sosnoff's historical trading edge was liquidity provision plus superior access and reaction speed. Business edge was trader-native product knowledge, rapid decisions, profitable operations, and content-led distribution.

**Main risks:**

- “Take as much risk as you can” is unsafe without position sizing, liquidity, runway, and ruin constraints.
- His survivorship makes concentrated bets look more universally repeatable than they are.
- Frequent manual trading can be engagement, not evidence of alpha; no audited performance is provided.
- Expected move describes market-implied distribution, not full tail or counterparty risk.
- Real estate critique is directionally useful but overgeneralized; leverage, local supply, cash yield, tax treatment, and holding horizon determine outcome.

## YK read

Best translation: maximize **attempt rate**, not probability of ruin. Concentrate effort where you hold real edge; keep financial positions liquid and survivable; build free cash flow; treat reputation as compounding capital. Sosnoff's life fits one-man-hedge-fund mindset, but his rhetoric needs explicit kill switches, exposure caps, and audited process.

## One-line summary

Take more liquid, probabilistic, survivable swings; build cash flow and reputation so one failure never ends game.
