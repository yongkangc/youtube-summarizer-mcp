# Why Tom Thinks Taking Risk Could Matter More Than Being Smart

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8GT98hccKNM

## TL;DR

Tom Sosnoff’s core claim: in trading and entrepreneurship, “bad trader” is less about making dumb trades and more about refusing reasonable risk for years. Smarts are table stakes; edge comes from knowing your risk identity, betting on yourself, staying in your domain, and being willing to act when others choose safety.

## Key points

- **Traders are not normal risk-takers.** He frames traders as impulsive, impatient, sometimes brilliant, sometimes stupid — but crucially willing to take risk and not emotionally destroyed by switching between those states.

- **Do it yourself.** His big personal lesson from 40 years of trading/investing: relying on other people to deliver returns, ideas, execution, or opportunity is hard. DIY investing and DIY business-building force ownership.

- **Stick to your domain.** He lists a long graveyard of outside-domain investments: fake oil wells, fake gas wells, mattress store, other traders, restaurants, gold mines in Mali, AI search, cannabis exchange, online textbooks, kids coding, investment property, storage units, pizza by the pound, pro wrestler. Lesson: he made money in trading, then lost large sums chasing businesses where he had no edge.

- **Seven trader ideologies.** His shorthand: `smarts`, `tells`, `adds`, `expect`, `lean`, `basis`, `indifference`.
  - You are not smarter than everyone.
  - There are no magical tells.
  - Do not blindly double down/add.
  - Keep reasonable expectations.
  - Do not lean on someone else to save you.
  - Reduce basis / improve probability.
  - Be indifferent across listed products because markets price them similarly.

- **Worst trade = not taking risk.** He argues most capable people are close enough in intelligence, passion, and desire. Differentiator is whether they take risk, assess risk, and act.

- **Bad trader definition.** Someone who repeatedly opts for safety/security over reasonable risk across years and situations. Not one prudent decision — a life pattern of avoiding variance.

- **Smart but safe is still average.** Examples: brilliant mathematician avoiding convention-challenge, lawyer clinging to partnership track, CEO scared to make deals, software developer afraid to risk 401k despite world-changing talent.

- **Opportunity comes from saying yes.** He says he always takes calls because opportunity is path-dependent. Recent callers were shocked because “nobody says yes anymore.” His point: being open to ideas itself is now unusual.

- **Risk-taker reaction to blowups differs.** On Voyager/Celsius/Three Arrows blowing up, he says risk-takers feel bad and want to “pick through the ruins”; risk-averse people feel vindicated they avoided it. He prefers opportunistic salvage over smug avoidance.

- **Know who you are.** His father’s advice: if you are a risk-taker, know that. Good risk-takers at least have chance to do something special; pure safety removes that possibility.

## Trading read-through

- **Theory of edge:** This is not a specific trade setup. It is process philosophy: risk-taking matters only when paired with domain edge, basis reduction, product indifference, and position/risk management. Otherwise “taking risk” becomes dumb variance.
- **Good part:** Strong reminder to avoid career/life over-optimization for safety, and to keep acting when risk is reasonable.
- **Danger:** Easy to misread as “take more risk.” Better translation: take *domain-valid, sized, repeatable* risk; avoid outsourced/ego investments where you have no edge.

## One-line summary

Smart is common; willing, domain-aware risk-taking is scarce — but only pays if you know your edge and size the downside.
