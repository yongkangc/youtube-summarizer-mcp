# Teacher Builds a Multi-Million-Dollar Options Portfolio

## TLDR

Former teacher Ross says he grew a roughly `$500K` passive-stock portfolio into “a couple million dollars” after adding active options trading around 2017. His process: persistent long bias, diversified short-premium structures, one-lot entries, roughly 1% initial position size, substantial cash/core-stock reserves, and thousands of repetitions—not home-run bets. Results are unaudited and attribution between bull-market stock gains, contributions and options P&L is unclear.

## Path

- Began investing roughly `$50/month` in mutual funds/stocks during 1990s.
- Continued buying through dot-com crash and 2008 crisis.
- Built portfolio to approximately `$500K` before learning options around 2016–2017.
- Started with short puts and covered calls against existing holdings.
- Expanded into defined-risk spreads, jade lizards, put ratio spreads, broken-wing butterflies, ETFs and futures products.
- Claims portfolio later reached “a couple million dollars” and enabled semi-retirement.
- Hosts estimate roughly `20%/year`; no account statements, cash-flow adjustment, benchmark comparison or drawdown history shown.

A fourfold gain implies approximately:

- `32.0% CAGR` over five years;
- `18.9%` over eight years;
- `16.7%` over nine years.

Therefore stated `4×`, “last half decade,” and `20%/year` cannot all describe same clean period without contributions/withdrawals.

## Current playbook

### Portfolio structure

- Long equity bias; believes in positive market drift.
- Approximately 40–50 positions simultaneously.
- Roughly 1,000 trades/year; about 4–5 per day.
- Around 30% of capital allocated to active trading.
- Roughly one-third in core stock positions; balance implied defensive/reserve capital.
- Starts around one lot or roughly 1% per trade, scales slowly.

### Preferred strategies

- Short puts
- Covered calls
- Covered strangles
- Jade lizards
- Put ratio spreads
- Broken-wing butterflies

Covered strangle example:

- Own 100 shares.
- Sell approximately 15–20 delta call.
- Sell approximately 15–20 delta put.
- Roll as needed.

This increases long exposure if stock falls because short put may assign another 100 shares. Covered call does not protect major downside.

He avoids naked calls after prior losses and treats 0DTE as engagement tool—not core strategy.

## Theory of edge

The portfolio combines two structural exposures:

1. **Equity risk premium:** remain long assets with positive long-run drift.
2. **Variance/insurance premium:** repeatedly sell options to investors paying for protection or convexity.

Market pays because trader absorbs gap risk, volatility expansion, assignment, negative convexity and liquidity/margin stress. “Consistency” alone is not edge; repetition compounds only if each strategy has positive expectancy after tails and costs.

## Strong lessons

- Start tiny; one-lot sizing protected him during sharp selloffs.
- Test structures before scaling.
- Use liquid products; illiquid holdings became memorable losers.
- Scale slower as wealth and lifestyle stakes grow.
- Move part of profits into defensive assets to secure family optionality.
- Own both successes and failures; avoid blaming market.
- Stock picking was not his edge—he says problems arose when he thought he “knew something.”
- Avoid reacting too quickly and getting whipsawed.

## Risk audit

### Performance evidence

- Returns are self-reported and unaudited.
- Initial `$500K` already came from decades of passive accumulation.
- Portfolio benefited from long equity exposure during major bull market.
- Contributions, withdrawals, taxes and exact dates omitted.
- “Essentially all through trading” is therefore not established.

### Hidden common factor

Short puts, jade lizards, covered strangles, ratio spreads and covered calls are not independent edges. Most combine:

- long equity beta;
- short volatility;
- short convexity;
- rising correlation during crashes.

Forty positions may still become one position under stress.

### High win rate

He emphasizes 90% winners/base hits. But high win rate can coexist with poor expectancy when occasional loss is much larger. Need evaluate:

- return on peak margin;
- worst gap loss;
- drawdown and recovery time;
- correlation during stress;
- assignment concentration;
- realized versus implied volatility after costs.

## YK read

Best lesson is not “teacher became rich selling options.” It is process discipline:

- compound slowly for decades;
- preserve core capital;
- begin every new strategy at minimum size;
- use options as overlay, not lottery;
- secure lifestyle before scaling risk;
- never confuse luck or bull beta with skill.

Before copying, demand audited decomposition of stock beta, deposits, option premium, realized losses and drawdowns.

## Bottom line

Plausible disciplined success story, not proof of a replicable 20% strategy. Durable edge comes from long-run equity and volatility premia plus small sizing; fatal risk is correlated short-convexity exposure masquerading as diversified base hits.
