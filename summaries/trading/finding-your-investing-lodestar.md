# Finding your Investing Lodestar: In Search of an Investment Philosophy!

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GpOzFKrnvdU

## TLDR

Damodaran argues that most active investors underperform not because they lack tactics, but because they lack a coherent investment philosophy. A good philosophy starts with beliefs about how markets make mistakes, then matches those beliefs to your temperament, time horizon, tax situation, liquidity needs, and edge.

## Key points

- **Investing needs a lodestar.** In uncertain markets, you need a stable set of beliefs about markets and investing so you do not get pulled around by every narrative, guru, or strategy.

- **An investment philosophy is not a strategy.** “Buy low PE stocks” is a strategy. The philosophy behind it might be: markets systematically over-punish boring or distressed companies. Strategy is the implementation; philosophy is the belief about why edge exists.

- **The default option is strong.** You can buy index funds, take market risk appropriate to your life, and likely beat most active investors. Damodaran stresses there is no shame in passive investing because roughly 90% of active investors underperform over long periods.

- **Successful investing records are hard to interpret.** Few investors beat the market consistently; even among winners, it is hard to separate luck from skill. Imitating famous investors usually fails because you copy the visible trades, not the hidden temperament, constraints, and timing.

- **No philosophy = vulnerable to scams.** Without a core view, greed drags you toward promises of 30–50% returns. A philosophy gives you the questions to ask before you get sold.

- **Strategies decay.** If a strategy works, others imitate it. Crowding and market correction reduce the excess return. A philosophy lets you adapt; a tactic alone leaves you stuck repeating something that stopped working.

- **All active investors must believe markets make systematic mistakes.** They can disagree on the mistake:
  - markets learn too slowly
  - markets overreact
  - markets misprice boring/distressed firms
  - markets mishandle information
  - similar assets trade at inconsistent prices
  These contradictory beliefs can coexist because markets are complex.

- **Major active investing families:**
  - **Trading on price/volume:** technical analysis, momentum, patterns.
  - **Trading on information:** earnings/news/events where prices underreact or overreact.
  - **Arbitrage/relative pricing:** similar assets mispriced across markets or instruments.
  - **Asset selection:** pick cheap/best securities within an asset class.
  - **Market timing:** shift between assets based on macro/fundamental/technical views.

- **Market timing has high payoff but low odds.** If you can time markets well, you can beat stock pickers easily. But Damodaran says it is much harder because you bring less unique edge and compete against everyone watching the same macro signals.

- **Execution costs matter.** Paper strategies often fail after costs. Costs include brokerage, bid-ask spread, and price impact. For illiquid stocks, spreads can be huge. For large investors, their own trades move prices.

- **Your philosophy must fit you.** You need to be honest about:
  - time horizon
  - tax constraints
  - liquidity needs
  - job stability
  - family obligations
  - patience
  - risk tolerance
  - size of capital
  Some strategies are right for someone else but wrong for you.

- **Philosophies must evolve.** Economic settings change. Damodaran highlights more winner-take-all markets, shorter company life cycles, greater crisis correlation across assets, and faster disruption. A 20th-century mean-reversion playbook may not work unchanged in the 21st century.

- **His own philosophy:**
  - He believes markets are mostly right most of the time.
  - He also believes markets make mistakes during uncertainty, complexity, crises, disruption, and life-cycle transitions.
  - He is primarily an intrinsic value investor.
  - He does not actively short much because timing and market mood can overwhelm valuation.
  - He passively times markets by holding more cash when uncomfortable, but avoids aggressive macro bets.
  - His blind spots: tax aversion and falling in love with his own company narratives.

## Practical takeaway for YK

Use this as a filter for your one-man hedge fund process:

- Before choosing a strategy, write the **market mistake** you believe exists.
- Then write why you personally can exploit it better than the average participant.
- Then check fit: time horizon, liquidity, tax, drawdown tolerance, attention bandwidth.
- Then define what would prove the strategy has decayed or your philosophy is wrong.

## One-line summary

Damodaran’s core message: do not collect strategies; build a philosophy about why markets misprice things, then only run strategies that fit both that belief and your own constraints.
