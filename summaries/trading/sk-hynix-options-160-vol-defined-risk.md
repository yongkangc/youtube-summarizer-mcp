# SK Hynix options: 160% IV and defined-risk spreads

[YouTube source](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d6JrPFGJae8)

## TL;DR

Tom Preston says newly listed SK Hynix ADR options are extremely active and very high IV, but margin treatment makes naked short options inefficient. His practical answer: **stick to tight front-week markets, sell volatility via defined-risk verticals, and avoid buying inflated premium**.

## Setup

- Underlying/ticker discussed: **SKHY**, newly listed SK Hynix ADR.
- Options began trading very soon after the ADR launch.
- Because it was first day of options trading, **open interest was zero intraday**; volume showed activity, but OI only updates after overnight clearing.
- Preston saw heavy first-day volume in near-dated strikes, including thousands of contracts around 140/150 puts and 180 calls.
- Near-week options had tighter markets, with spreads around 5–15 cents in some strikes. Further expiries were wider.

## IV / vol point

- SKHY 3-day implied volatility was around **160%**.
- Preston compares it to SpaceX options, which launched above **200% short-term IV** and later fell toward roughly **84%**.
- His concern: if SKHY IV compresses similarly, long options can lose even if directional view is not disastrous.
- Therefore he would not buy naked calls/puts into this IV. He would rather be net short premium in defined-risk form.

## Mechanics shown

### Naked short put

Example: sell **160P** for about **$2.00 / $200 credit**.

- Buying power requirement: about **$15,790**.
- Effective return on buying power: **1.27%**.
- Broker/exchange treated position close to cash-secured despite margin account.
- Preston rejects it as poor capital efficiency: too much capital tied up for too little premium.

### Bullish defined-risk trade

Example: sell **160P / buy 155P** short put vertical.

- Width: **$5 / $500**.
- Credit: about **$0.63 / $63**.
- Max loss: about **$437**.
- Buying power: about **$440**.
- Return on buying power: about **14.3%**.
- Breakeven: **159.37**.

### Bearish defined-risk trade

Example: sell **200C / buy 205C** short call vertical.

- Width: **$5 / $500**.
- Credit: about **$1.05 / $105**.
- Max loss: **$395**.
- Return on buying power: about **26.6%**.
- Breakeven: **201.05**.

## Edge and risk

**Theory of edge:** Sell overpriced implied volatility after new-option launch while limiting tail exposure via verticals. Edge, if any, is volatility-risk premium plus temporary launch-dislocation pricing, not directional prediction.

Main risks:

- First-day options have no OI history; volume can be noisy and market makers are still discovering prices.
- IV can stay high or expand if underlying gaps again.
- Vertical spreads cap risk but also cap profit and can still lose max quickly in a fast ADR move.
- Wide/worsening bid-ask outside front-week can eat edge.
- ADR borrow, positioning, and inability to short underlying can push options flow into distorted demand.
- SKHY may remain more volatile than SpaceX if AI/memory cycle narrative stays hot.

## YK read

This is a clean process note: high IV alone does not mean sell naked. If buying power is cash-secured and underlying can gap, naked premium sale may be bad ROC. Convert view into vertical: choose direction, define max loss, collect enough credit to justify width, and avoid paying extreme IV unless you have strong catalyst timing.

## One-line summary

In newly listed high-IV options, sell rich vol only with defined risk; do not buy premium or tie up cash-secured capital for tiny yield.
