# The New Fed Chair will Shock Markets!

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rIvrWTvXK0I

## TL;DR

The speaker argues that a new Fed chair, “Kevin Walsh” in the transcript, would push aggressive rate cuts by claiming AI productivity creates structural deflation. His portfolio implication: stay invested in quality asset-owning / compounder equities, avoid long-duration bonds and inflation-squeezed cyclicals, and don’t hide fully in cash.

## Key points

- Market backdrop:
  - US stocks keep making new highs.
  - S&P 500 quoted as up over 8% YTD.
  - Speaker says his own portfolio is up 16% YTD and 4.51% in the last month.
  - He attributes the rally to:
    - Stronger-than-expected corporate earnings.
    - Market expectations around the new Fed chair and rate cuts.

- Fed policy thesis:
  - The speaker says Powell resisted cutting rates because inflation remained sticky.
  - He says the new chair has been framing the case for aggressive cuts.
  - Claimed new logic: AI productivity changes the inflation/growth relationship.
  - Traditional model:
    - Strong growth → inflation risk → Fed holds/raises rates.
  - New AI-deflation model:
    - AI lets companies produce more with fewer employees/resources.
    - Productivity rises.
    - Overheads and costs fall.
    - Companies can eventually lower prices.
    - Therefore, strong growth can coexist with falling inflation.
  - Speaker’s caveat: current CPI/PPI are still sticky and higher than expected, so this is a preemptive bet, not confirmed data.

## Winners if aggressive cuts happen

- Growth / tech stocks should benefit most:
  - Lower discount rates boost long-duration growth equities.
  - AI-driven margin expansion adds another tailwind.
  - Names mentioned:
    - Nvidia
    - Google / Alphabet
    - Palantir
    - Palo Alto Networks

## Losers / risk zones

- Deep cyclicals and physical-cost-heavy businesses:
  - If rates are cut but inflation stays high, input costs remain painful.
  - These companies may not benefit quickly from AI productivity.
  - Margins get squeezed by energy, commodities, logistics, and labor costs.
  - Examples mentioned:
    - FedEx
    - UPS
    - Delta / airlines broadly
    - Kroger
    - Dow Chemical
    - Ford
    - General Motors
    - Target
    - Carnival Cruise Lines

- Long-duration bonds:
  - Bearish for TLT / long-term Treasuries.
  - Reason: if bond market thinks Fed is cutting too fast into inflation or energy shock, long-end yields can rise even as Fed cuts short rates.
  - Fed controls the short end, not the long end.

- Short/medium duration bonds:
  - More positive for 1–5 year duration bonds.
  - They capture Fed cuts without as much exposure to long-term inflation repricing.

## Portfolio logic

The speaker frames two scenarios:

- Scenario 1: the new Fed chair is right.
  - AI productivity brings structural deflation.
  - Fed cuts rates.
  - Stocks rise.
  - Inflation falls.
  - Consumers and investors both win.

- Scenario 2: the new Fed chair is wrong.
  - Fed cuts too early.
  - Oil/geopolitical shocks keep inflation high.
  - Stocks and real assets may still rise nominally.
  - Cash holders lose purchasing power.

His conclusion: in both scenarios, it is better to own assets than sit entirely in cash.

  - He would consider scaling out only if it were grossly overvalued, e.g. more than 100% above intrinsic value.

- Palo Alto Networks:
  - Intrinsic value quoted: $199.
  - Current price quoted: $260.
  - Overvalued and overextended short term.

  - Same logic applied to cybersecurity names he owns: Palo Alto, Fortinet, CrowdStrike.

## Practical takeaway

- Don’t blindly buy everything because liquidity is rising.
- Avoid bubble-like “shiny objects” and overhyped stocks.
- Prefer quality compounders that are fair-valued or undervalued.
- If holding great companies that are only moderately overvalued, long-term investors may keep holding rather than churn.
- Sell decisions depend on objective:
  - Need cash / short-term trader → selling overvalued names can make sense.
  - Long-term compounder investor → hold unless valuation becomes extreme.

## My read

This is basically an “asset inflation beats cash” argument dressed in AI-deflation/Fed-cut language. The strongest part is the distinction between short-end cuts and long-end inflation risk; the weaker part is assuming aggressive cuts are bullish for equities even if inflation re-accelerates — nominal prices may rise, but real returns and multiples can still get hit if long yields spike.

## One-line summary

If the Fed cuts aggressively on an AI-deflation thesis, the speaker wants quality growth equities and short-duration bonds, not cash, long-duration bonds, or inflation-squeezed cyclicals.
