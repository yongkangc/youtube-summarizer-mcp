# Tom Sosnoff: 11 Boring Options Strategies

[Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z__VENA80Bo)

## TL;DR

Sosnoff's playbook is mostly short premium: enter around 30–50 DTE, prefer elevated implied-volatility rank, sell strikes near expected move, take profit around 50% of credit, diversify underlyings, and control size.

His two biggest claimed profit sources are:

1. short strangles;
2. contrarian volatility/mean-reversion trades.

The structural edge is variance risk premium: sellers receive compensation for warehousing volatility, gap, and crash risk. High win rate is not enough. These strategies can hide severe negative skew and correlated margin expansion.

## Theory of edge

> Option buyers often overpay for protection and convexity, allowing disciplined sellers to harvest implied volatility above subsequent realized volatility.

Market pays seller because seller absorbs losses during crashes, jumps, volatility clustering, and liquidity stress. Edge requires premium to exceed tail losses, spreads, commissions, financing, and capital drag over full cycles.

## Common operating rules

- Typical entry: 35–50 DTE.
- Prefer high IV rank, often at least ~30; higher for neutral short-volatility trades.
- Short strikes around expected move, commonly 16–22 delta.
- Close around 50% of maximum credit.
- Enter/exit multi-leg structures as one order; avoid legging.
- Undefined-risk positions require small size, excess capital, and low cross-position correlation.

## Eleven strategies

### 1. Short put — bullish

- Sell OTM put around 35–50 DTE.
- Target 16–22 delta near expected move.
- Prefer beaten-down stock with IV rank ≥30.
- Take profit around 50% of credit.

Highest-conviction bullish default. Undefined downside and concentrated crash risk remain.

### 2. Jade lizard — bullish

Short put plus short call spread. Structure combined credit above call-spread width so upside cannot lose at expiry. Around 40 DTE; take 50% of credit.

Adds premium/downside buffer but retains naked-put downside.

### 3. Covered call — mildly bullish

Long stock plus 25–30 delta short call, usually 40–60 DTE. Best on cheaper, high-IV stocks. Close structure together; consider profit-taking if short strike breached.

Covered call reduces basis but sacrifices upside; downside remains nearly stock-like.

### 4. Short put spread — bullish, defined risk

Target credit around 30–35% of spread width; close around 50% of credit. Used when naked put consumes excessive buying power.

Defined max loss, but narrow spreads can have poor reward-to-risk and difficult adjustment.

### 5. Put ratio spread — bullish, undefined risk

Typically buy one put and sell two farther-OTM puts for net credit, positioned beyond expected move. High win rate; severe downside below lower strike.

Not “additional protection” in tail. Long put protects moderate decline while extra naked short put reintroduces crash exposure.

### 6. Short call spread — bearish, defined risk

Sell call spread inside expected move, around 30–50 DTE. Target 30–40% of width and 50% profit. Used for expensive stocks and reducing portfolio long delta.

He argues call spreads can trade richer than put spreads due to upside call skew; this is asset/regime dependent, not universal.

### 7. Call broken-wing butterfly — bearish

Credit 1:-2:1 skip-strike structure around one month. Long strike near expected move; farther wing outside it. Used for earnings or runaway stocks.

Can potentially buy back embedded vertical and retain butterfly, but payoff remains path-, strike-, and liquidity-sensitive.

### 8. Unbalanced iron condor — bearish tilt

Wider call spread plus narrower put spread, e.g. $10 call wing and $5 put wing. Defined risk, short premium, net short delta. Around 40 DTE; close near 50% of credit.

### 9. Iron condor — neutral, defined risk

Defined-risk short strangle. Target combined credit around 30–40% of wing width; close after 30–50% of credit. Best when IV rank high and underlying stays range-bound.

Difficult to adjust; wing cost and commissions can consume volatility premium.

### 10. Short strangle — neutral short volatility

His main strategy:

- sell 16–20 delta put and call;
- target ~45 DTE;
- prefer very high IV rank;
- close around 50% of credit;
- roll untested side to manage delta;
- diversify across non-correlated underlyings.

Largest risks: oversizing, low-IV entry, gap exposure, correlation convergence, and margin expansion during crashes.

### 11. Volatility mean reversion / pairs trading

Two variants:

- sell elevated implied volatility expecting contraction;
- trade spread between correlated products using volatility-adjusted notionals, often through futures.

Pairs reduce common market exposure, not necessarily 80% of total risk. Relationship can structurally break.

## What is useful

- Explicit structure selection by directional view and capital constraints.
- Repeated entry-duration and profit-taking rules.
- Strong warning against oversizing undefined-risk positions.
- Recognition that high IV and diversification matter.
- Preference for package execution instead of legging.

## Problems and caveats

- “Volatility must come down” is overstated. Volatility can remain elevated, increase further, or shift regime.
- Probability-of-profit estimates are model/market implied, not guaranteed realized frequencies.
- High win rate says nothing about expectancy without loss severity.
- Rolling untested side manages delta but can increase total credit and duration while preserving tail risk.
- Apparent diversification disappears when correlations spike.
- No audited P&L, drawdowns, leverage, margin usage, or tail-year decomposition supports the 70% profit claim.
- “Boring” does not mean low risk. Most structures sell convexity.

## YK read

Useful menu, dangerous if copied as strategy. All structures largely express same latent bet: **short volatility/short convexity**. Eleven tickets do not create eleven independent edges.

Before use, verify:

1. implied minus realized volatility after costs;
2. crash and gap expectancy;
3. portfolio correlation under stress;
4. buying-power expansion;
5. executable fill/exit assumptions;
6. return on peak—not initial—margin.

Best lesson: small size + high IV + disciplined exits. Worst lesson: confusing high probability of profit with safe or positive expectancy.
