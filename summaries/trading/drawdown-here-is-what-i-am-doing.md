# Drawdown?!? Here is What I Am Doing

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eVnKlL6HIuo

## TL;DR

Adam Khoo says drawdowns are not evidence that a quality-growth portfolio is broken; they are a structural feature of owning equities, especially contrarian/high-quality compounders. His response: do not rotate into this year's winners just because they are working; audit whether each holding's fundamentals and moat are intact, add to high-quality names when the decline is cyclical, and avoid expensive hedging/timing games.

## Key points

- His portfolio is shown down about **11.05% YTD** for the first three months, versus the S&P 500 around **-9%**. He says the number is distorted by moving shares into a new corporate account; on the original account, the drawdown was closer to **-7.67%**.

- He explains the account move as estate-tax planning: he transferred about **$5.8m** of shares from a joint account into a corporate account because a company/trust does not die, avoiding potential US estate-tax exposure.

- His portfolio is more exposed to communication services and consumer discretionary/cyclicals. These sectors normally outperform over long periods because of higher growth, margins, moats, and returns on capital, but can underperform sharply during fear cycles.

- He argues the current year is “bizarro”: energy, materials, utilities, and consumer defensives are supporting the market, while his preferred higher-quality growth sectors are weak due to fears like AI disruption and geopolitical/oil shocks.

- His answer is not to chase what is currently working. He expects mean reversion: when oil/geopolitical fear fades, defensive/commodity leadership may reverse and high-quality growth should recover.

- Historical reminder from his own portfolio: over seven years, he says it returned about **267%** versus the S&P 500's **175%**, but suffered major drawdowns along the way: **-36%** in COVID, **-26%** in 2022, **-19%** around tariff panic, and now around **-11%**.

- Buffett and Peter Lynch are used as examples: Berkshire compounded far ahead of the S&P but had repeated large drawdowns, including **-51%**, **-54%**, **-31%**, etc. Lynch's Magellan Fund compounded around **29%/yr**, but also had big annual drawdowns.

- Key behavioral point: more than half of Magellan investors allegedly lost money despite the fund's amazing record because they bought after strong runs and sold during drawdowns. Good vehicle + bad behavior = bad outcome.

- He cites market-history research: the median maximum drawdown across US stocks from 1985–2024 was about **85%**. Even the top wealth creators — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Alphabet, Amazon, Exxon — had average maximum drawdowns around **80%**.

- Amazon example: down **95%** in 1999–2001, later became a multi-trillion-dollar company; also down **56%** in 2022 and **23%** this year. Huge drawdowns can coexist with huge long-term compounding.

- Wes Gray's “Even God Would Get Fired” point: even a perfect-foresight portfolio of future winners would still suffer a **76%** drawdown and multiple **30%** drawdowns. Drawdown alone does not prove thesis failure.

- But he is not saying “hold anything forever.” He says **99% of stocks are uninvestable**: inconsistent revenue/profit/free cash flow, high debt, weak margins, low return on capital, no moat.

- The real distinction: cyclical vs secular damage.
  - Cyclical: short-term fear, temporary earnings miss, macro shock; hold/add if fundamentals remain strong.
  - Secular: moat destruction, revenue/profit/free-cash-flow deterioration, competitive advantage disappearing; cut and redeploy.

- Microsoft is his example of a hold/add candidate: price weak, but revenue, operating income, and cash flow still growing.

- Kraft Heinz is his example of avoid/cut: share price weakness accompanied by deteriorating fundamentals, declining revenue, weaker operating income, and weaker free cash flow.

- He warns against hindsight market timing: “I should have sold a month ago” is dangerous because it trains you to exit after every wave up. Many times the market keeps running and you never get back in.

- He says he used to jump in/out of great companies, made money, but would have been much richer if he simply held the great companies through volatility.

- On hedging: he rarely hedges now. Puts, shorts, and inverse ETFs cost money. Maybe **1 out of 5** times the hedge pays off; often the market reverses and the hedge drags returns.

- He cites Buffett's hedge-fund bet: selected hedge funds returned roughly **21%, 42%, 87%, 2.8%, 27%** over 10 years versus the S&P 500's **125%**. His point: hedging may reduce volatility, but often lowers long-run returns.

- He also cites recent hedge-fund average returns around **6.7%** versus buy-and-hold S&P around **14.5%** over a 5–6 year period.

- Midterm-election year framing: average S&P 500 drawdown in midterm years since 1950 is about **16%**. If the market is only down **9%**, more downside would not be surprising. Historically, he says markets posted double-digit gains one year after every midterm-year drawdown.

## Practical checklist

- Do not judge portfolio quality by one drawdown.
- Re-underwrite each holding:
  - revenue growth intact?
  - operating income growing?
  - free cash flow growing?
  - debt conservative?
  - margins/ROIC still high?
  - moat intact or weakening?
- If fundamentals intact and drawdown is cyclical: hold/add.
- If fundamentals deteriorating structurally: cut and redeploy.
- Do not rotate into low-quality “current winners” just because they are outperforming this quarter.
- Avoid reflexive hedging unless your objective is lower volatility rather than maximum long-run return.
- Keep cash ready for quality names during broad fear.

## My read

Sensible core message: drawdown tolerance is the price of equity compounding. The useful part is the cyclical-vs-secular underwriting test. The weaker part is the blanket anti-hedging tone — for concentrated portfolios, hedging can be rational if it prevents forced selling or protects against true tail risk. But as a default retail behavior, he is right: most hedging becomes expensive market timing.

## One-line summary

A quality portfolio being down is not the problem; failing to distinguish temporary price pain from permanent thesis damage is the problem.
