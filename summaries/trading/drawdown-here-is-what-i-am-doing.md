# Drawdown?!? Here is What I Am Doing.

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eVnKlL6HIuo

## TL;DR

Adam Khoo says portfolio drawdowns are not a sign you are wrong; they are the price of owning equities. His rule: hold and add to high-quality companies when fundamentals are intact, but cut companies where the business is in secular decline.

## Key points

- Current drawdown:
  - His portfolio is down about 11.05% YTD in the new corporate account.
  - He says the comparable original account is down about 7.67%, slightly better than the S&P 500’s roughly 9% drawdown.
  - The difference is due to account migration timing: he moved about $5.8M of shares into a corporate account on 14 Jan to reduce US estate-tax risk.

- Why his portfolio can underperform short term:
  - He owns mostly communication services, consumer discretionary/cyclicals, and high-growth compounders.
  - Many of these are down double digits: Microsoft, Nvidia, Meta, Palo Alto.
  - S&P 500 is being supported by energy stocks, which benefit from higher oil prices.
  - He avoids many sectors now outperforming — energy, basic materials, utilities, consumer defensives — because he thinks they have thinner margins, lower returns on capital, higher debt, and weaker long-term compounding.
  - His view: don’t rotate into what just worked; stick to high-quality companies and expect mean reversion.

## Drawdowns are normal

- His own 7-year portfolio history:
  - 2019–2026: portfolio up about 267% vs S&P 500 up about 175%.
  - Drawdowns along the way:
    - COVID 2020: down about 36%.
    - 2022 Fed hiking bear market: down about 26%.
    - 2025 tariff panic: down about 19%.
  - Point: even outperforming portfolios regularly suffer double-digit drawdowns.

- Warren Buffett / Berkshire example:
  - Berkshire has compounded far ahead of the S&P over decades.
  - But it had repeated major drawdowns: around 51%, 54%, 31%, 27%, and many 18–24% drops.
  - Even Buffett cannot avoid drawdowns.

- Peter Lynch / Magellan example:
  - Magellan returned about 604% over Lynch’s 13 years vs S&P 500 about 223%.
  - CAGR quoted: about 29%.
  - But the fund had repeated large drawdowns: 56%, 42%, 32%, 27%.
  - More than 50% of investors in the fund still lost money because they bought after strong runs and sold during drawdowns.
  - Lesson: you can own the best fund or best stocks and still lose if you buy high and sell low.

## Psychology is the edge

- He says around 70% of investment success is psychology.
- Drawdowns create the emotional test:
  - New investors see good companies falling and assume something is wrong.
  - The pain tempts them to sell at the worst point.
  - The winning behavior is to distinguish price volatility from business impairment.

## Research cited

- Morgan Stanley / Michael Mauboussin drawdown research:
  - Drawdowns are the “price of admission” for long-term equity returns.
  - Median maximum drawdown across 6,500 US stocks from 1985–2024: about 85%.
  - Even top wealth creators — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Alphabet, Amazon, Exxon Mobil — had average maximum drawdown around 80%.
  - Amazon examples quoted:
    - Down 95% from 1999–2001, then became a multi-trillion-dollar company.
    - Down 56% in 2022.
    - Down 33% in 2024/2025 periods.
    - Down about 23% YTD in the discussed period.
    - Long-term return quoted: about 258,000% vs S&P 500 about 567%.

- Wes Gray “Even God Would Get Fired” point:
  - Even with perfect foresight of the best-performing stocks over the next five years, the portfolio would still suffer a 76% drawdown and five separate 30% drawdowns.
  - Implication: drawdowns are structural to equity ownership, not automatic evidence of bad judgment.

## What to hold vs what to sell

- Do not blindly hold everything.
- Most stocks are not investable, in his view:
  - No consistent revenue/profit/free-cash-flow growth.
  - High debt.
  - Low margins.
  - Low return on capital.
  - Weak/no economic moat.

- Hold / add when:
  - Company has strong moat.
  - Revenue, operating income, and free cash flow are still growing.
  - Debt is conservative.
  - Price drop is caused by cyclical or short-term fears.
  - Example: Microsoft — stock price down but fundamentals still growing.

- Sell / cut when:
  - Business is in secular decline.
  - Revenue and profits are deteriorating.
  - Competitive advantage is being destroyed.
  - Free cash flow is inconsistent.
  - Example: Kraft Heinz — declining fundamentals; Berkshire eventually cut loss.

## Why he does not try to time exits

- He warns against hindsight regret: “I should have sold before the drop and bought back lower.”
- Problem: if you sell every time after a big wave up, you miss the largest compounding runs.
- He says he used to jump in and out of great companies, made some money, but would be much richer if he had simply held through volatility.
- Lesson: for great companies, time in market beats trying to dodge every wave down.

## Why he does not hedge much anymore

- Hedging tools mentioned:
  - Put options.
  - Shorting market.
  - Inverse ETFs.
- His view: hedging is not free.
  - Maybe 1 out of 5 times the market falls further and hedges pay off.
  - But 4 out of 5 times the market rebounds and hedges lose money.
  - Long term, hedging can reduce returns.

- Buffett hedge-fund bet:
  - 2008–2017: hedge funds/funds of funds returned about 21%, 42%, 87%, 2.8%, 27% depending on fund.
  - S&P 500 returned about 125%.
  - Point: lower volatility often comes at cost of lower long-term returns.

- More recent research quoted:
  - Hedge funds averaged about 6.7% vs buy-and-hold S&P about 14.5% over a recent 5–6 year period.

## What he is doing now

- “Suck it up” emotionally.
- If you have spare cash and not full positions, use drawdowns to add to great companies.
- Do not abandon the plan because current year sector leadership looks strange.
- Focus on business quality and whether the drawdown is cyclical vs secular.

## Midterm election year point

- He says midterm election years are historically choppy.
- Average S&P 500 drawdown in midterm years since 1950: about 16%.
- Current drawdown discussed: about 9%, so more downside would not be surprising.
- But historically, one year after midterm-year drawdowns, the market had double-digit gains every time, according to him.

## My read

This is mainly a behavioral investing note. The useful operating rule is not “never sell”; it is “don’t sell price drawdowns unless the business thesis is impaired.” That is the right distinction. The weak spot is survivorship bias: citing Amazon/Microsoft/Nvidia drawdowns is useful, but only if you also have a process that avoids the many stocks that draw down 80–95% and never recover.

## One-line summary

Drawdowns are the entry fee for long-term equity compounding: hold/add great businesses when fundamentals are intact, but cut secular losers and don’t waste too much return trying to hedge every correction.
