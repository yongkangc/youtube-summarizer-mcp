# Don't Rely on SPY and Tom's Trades of the Week

## TLDR

Tom Sosnoff argues SPY is increasingly a concentrated mega-cap bet rather than complete diversification, while individual stocks remain useful for direct directional exposure. His four weekly ideas sell elevated or asymmetric option premium in natural gas, SMH and META, plus a defined-risk bullish Microsoft broken-wing butterfly. Main caveat: high probability of profit and margin ROC conceal undefined tail risk, margin expansion and pin-dependent payouts.

## SPY concentration

- Major benchmarks have become increasingly concentrated; roughly ten companies can represent around 40% of index exposure.
- Historical program traders could replicate approximately 95–96% of S&P behavior with roughly 100 stocks; today fewer names dominate.
- Concentration has helped during bull market but reduces diversification exactly when mega-cap leadership reverses.
- SPY remains useful market beta, but should not be mistaken for broad independent-factor diversification.

## Covered-call management

When covered call works and stock is called away:

1. Do not reflexively roll.
2. Close stock and short call together; realize profit.
3. If still bullish, sell an out-of-the-money put.

Tom claims this can reduce capital requirement from roughly 50% to 20% and raise probability of profit from 60–65% toward 80%. Those numbers are broker/margin/strike dependent. Covered call and cash-secured short put are economically similar via put-call parity.

## Four trades of week

### 1. Natural gas bullish short put

At `/NG` around `2.84–2.85`, approximately 44 DTE:

- Sell August `2.55` put, approximately 19 delta.
- Credit approximately `$190` per contract.
- Buying power quoted around `$1,900`.
- Platform POP approximately 80%.
- IV rank low; thesis is directional mean reversion after selloff, not volatility premium.

**Edge claim:** Natural gas oversold; upside-skewed calls make downside put relatively cheap, but strike sits outside expected move and futures liquidity permits hedging.

**Risk:** 10% “return on capital” uses entry margin, not maximum loss. Margin can expand sharply. At zero, approximate loss is `$25,310`; negative futures prices could make loss worse. Annualizing one 45-day margin ROC into 75–80% assumes every trade repeats without losses, margin shocks or idle periods—invalid as expected return.

## Options flow and market structure

- Retail semiconductor option premium reportedly reached about `$1.9B/day` on cited platform in June, six times historical average; 75% calls.
- Speakers reject broad claim that options permanently “wag the stock-market dog.” Dealers hedge delta, but deep markets usually absorb flow.
- Large individual orders can move underlying temporarily; effects often mean-revert.
- Options growth mainly improves liquidity, symbol breadth and access.
- Their view is asserted from practitioner experience, not demonstrated causal study.

## Buybacks

- Roughly `$1T` of 2026 authorizations/execution discussed as major market support.
- Authorization does not guarantee purchase.
- Firms often buy more in rising markets and pause during volatility—opposite ideal value behavior.
- Buybacks may be preferred over dividends/acquisitions when management sees few better uses of cash, but can still destroy value if shares are overpriced.

## Stocks in active portfolio

Tom's allocation:

- Stocks: under 5–10% of trades.
- Yet consume roughly 20–25% buying power because stock is capital intensive.
- Uses:
  - pure directional exposure;
  - covered calls or short-stock/short-put covered writes;
  - occasional after-hours delta adjustment after earnings;
  - Treasury ETFs such as BIL/SGOV;
  - passive positive-drift exposure.

Stock selection can diversify away from index concentration, but adds idiosyncratic risk and requires actual information/valuation edge.

## Bottom line

Episode's durable lessons: SPY is beta, not complete diversification; stock positions consume disproportionate capital; and option POP/ROC metrics must be translated into full payoff and stress margin. Weekly ideas are premium-selling/mean-reversion trades—not free high-probability returns.
