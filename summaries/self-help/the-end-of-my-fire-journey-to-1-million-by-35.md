# The End of My FIRE Journey to $1 Million by 35

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3O_1S8YBweQ

## TLDR

Singapore personal finance creator reflects on hitting his old goal: **US$1m net worth by age 35**, actually reached at **34**. The useful lesson is less “copy my number” and more: set a clear target, model real life costs, build active income aggressively, invest passively, and then decide whether FIRE is really about quitting or about doing work you actually want to keep doing.

## Main points

- He set the goal at **30 in 2021**: reach **US$1m by 35**.
  - Logic was the **4% rule**: US$40k/year, about US$3,333/month, then roughly S$4,500/month.
  - He frames this as a security floor, not luxury.

- Critics said the goal was unrealistic because he had not yet paid for major life milestones:
  - marriage
  - house / BTO
  - renovation
  - future family commitments

- His response: those were already in his spreadsheet assumptions.
  - He later got married in **2023**.
  - Got a **4-room HDB BTO**.
  - Paid down payment via CPF.
  - Paid full renovation in cash, no loan.
  - Says there was **zero parent support**.

- Career path:
  - Graduated in **2015**, started as corporate accountant.
  - Worked about **6 years**, got chartered accountant qualification.
  - Around **2021**, moved within company to a role with more time flexibility so he could build YouTube as a side hustle.
  - First year of YouTube made almost no money, but salary gave runway.

- 2022 bear market was psychologically hard.
  - Portfolio was heavy in US tech/growth.
  - Saw repeated four-figure / five-figure losses.
  - Did not sell core portfolio, especially Mag 7-style holdings.
  - Continued investing CPF/SRS through platforms like Endowus, POEMS, StashAway.

- In **Jan 2023**, he quit to do YouTube full-time.
  - Gave up salary, CPF contributions, employee benefits, annual leave.
  - Framed the risk as acceptable: if it failed, he could go back to corporate work.
  - In **Sep 2023**, his wife also left her job to join the business, increasing single-business concentration risk.

- He tested semi-retirement in Malaysia for one year.
  - Lower cost of living due to SGD/MYR and lower local costs.
  - But income/motivation fell because the environment was more relaxed.
  - His conclusion: good for retirement/living, not ideal for pushing business income.

- Renovation/HDB phase:
  - Got BTO keys in **Aug 2024**.
  - Used renovation content to show practical, budget-aware home setup rather than six-figure luxury renovation.
  - Paid renovation from cash buffer without selling investments.

- Net worth composition:
  - Mostly equity funds, majority broad-based diversified index funds.
  - Some individual stocks, but not the main driver.
  - CPF compounded from first work contribution, plus voluntary top-ups.
  - Hit FRS around **2021**.
  - Does **not** include own-stay HDB appreciation in net worth.

- Emotional reality of hitting US$1m:
  - No dramatic life change.
  - More relief than celebration: “the math worked.”
  - Felt like a report card, not an endpoint.

## FIRE vs Ikigai

- FIRE = making work optional; some people really want to stop working.
- Ikigai = work that overlaps what you enjoy, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what can make money.
- His conclusion: if stopping the work you like makes you more miserable, why stop?
- The goal was not to quit forever, but to build a life/business he does not need to escape from.

## Practical takeaways

- Model real life costs, not fantasy FIRE numbers.
  - Wedding, house, renovation, family commitments need to be in the spreadsheet.

- Build active income actively, passive income passively.
  - His wealth was accelerated by business income, not only investment returns.
  - Investments were mostly passive/index-style compounding.

- Keep buffers before taking career risk.
  - He only quit after side hustle traction and emergency runway.
  - Wife joining the business increased risk, so runway mattered more.

- FIRE number is personal.
  - US$1m may be enough for one person, too low for another, excessive for someone else.
  - Depends on lifestyle, family, liabilities, starting point, and desired geography.

- The system matters more than the number.
  - Accountability, income growth, saving discipline, investing consistency, and lifestyle design are the real drivers.

## My read for YK

The most useful frame is: FIRE is not the prize; **optionality is the prize**. His path worked because he combined clear financial modeling, aggressive active-income building, passive compounding, and controlled life-cost buffers. But the deeper move is Ikigai: use the capital base to keep doing work you would still choose even when you no longer have to.

## One-line summary

US$1m by 35 was the scoreboard, but the real win was building a life where work became chosen, not forced.
