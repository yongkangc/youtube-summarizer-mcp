# Breaking Down My $306,500 Salary in San Francisco

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7VwEOyAcvJM

## TLDR

Raymond Zeng is a Meta software engineer earning average annual compensation of **$306,500** in the San Francisco Bay Area. He keeps fixed living costs low, maximizes tax-advantaged accounts, invests roughly **80% US / 20% international**, and targets financial independence by age 30. The video does **not disclose a verified current total net worth**; it discloses savings flows, selected cash reserves, and projected future balances.

## Compensation and cash flow

- Employer: Meta
- Role: software engineer; work includes coding, project/product management, and data analysis
- Hours: **40–50/week**
- Average annual compensation: **$306,500**
- Gross monthly equivalent: about **$25,542**
- Cash take-home after taxes and retirement contributions: about **$4,000/month**
- Other income: none
- Monthly savings/investments: **$5,000–$20,000**, depending on stock vesting, bonuses, and whether his 401(k) is already maxed

The $4,000 take-home and $5,000–$20,000 savings are not contradictory: much compensation arrives through stock, bonuses, and retirement contributions rather than ordinary spendable paycheck cash.

## Monthly spending

- Rent: **$2,600**
- Utilities: **$150–$170**
- Transport: usually **$0**, occasionally up to **$30**
  - no car
  - walks, uses BART/rideshare, and takes company shuttle to work
- Groceries: **$300**
- Eating out: budget up to **$75**, usually below that
- Hobbies/travel/furry community: **$400–$500**
- Credit-card annual fees: about **$1,000/year**, equivalent to **$83/month**
- Health insurance: **$17/month**
- Known recurring spend: approximately **$3,550–$3,775/month**, before irregular travel and major purchases
- Cash left from stated $4,000 take-home: roughly **$225–$450/month** in an ordinary month

Employer subsidizes breakfast and lunch. He cooks almost all dinners at home and deliberately keeps the apartment sparse: no couch, no television, filing cabinet as nightstand.

## Savings and investments

- Employee pretax 401(k) contribution: **$24,500/year**
- Uses after-tax 401(k) contributions toward the **$72,000** total IRS plan limit, including employer contributions
- Says roughly **one-sixth of compensation** goes into the 401(k)
- Maxes Roth IRA annually
- Maxes HSA annually
- Remaining surplus goes to taxable brokerage
- Portfolio allocation:
  - **80% US equities**
  - **20% international equities**

Selected earmarked cash visible in his spreadsheet:

- Emergency fund: approximately **$32,000**
- Cash reserved for future fursuit: **$14,000**
- Fursuit expected cost: generally **$4,000–$7,000**

These are not a complete balance sheet.

## How much does he currently have?

**Exact current net worth is not stated.** Do not confuse projection with present assets.

What the video establishes:

- at least **$32,000 emergency cash**
- another **$14,000 earmarked for fursuit**
- unknown balances in 401(k), Roth IRA, HSA, and brokerage
- saves/invests **$5,000–$20,000 monthly**
- projected investments of **north of $2 million by age 30**, assuming market and contribution assumptions hold
- projected investments of roughly **$7 million+ around age 40**

Therefore, only defensible current lower bound from clearly visible earmarked reserves is about **$46,000**, plus undisclosed retirement and brokerage assets. That is not his net worth.

## FIRE plan

- Retirement target: **$1.6 million** by age 30
- At 4%, that supports about **$64,000/year** or **$5,333/month**
- He adds cushion because retiring before 40 creates longer sequence-of-returns risk
- Spreadsheet assumes approximately **7% post-inflation returns**
- He does not expect to stop all work; expects occasional contracting, travel, and conventions
- Future partner/children would require reworking target

## Practical read

- Main edge is compensation, not grocery optimization. Meta pay plus stock vesting creates savings capacity.
- Sparse lifestyle prevents lifestyle inflation from consuming high income.
- Low cash paycheck is intentional because capital is routed into retirement accounts and equity compensation.
- Projection risk is substantial: continued Meta compensation, stock vesting, high savings, and equity returns all need to persist.
- FIRE number should be treated as optionality target, not guaranteed retirement date.

## One-line summary

He earns $306,500, spends roughly $3,550–$3,775 monthly, saves/invests $5,000–$20,000 monthly, has at least $46,000 in disclosed earmarked cash plus undisclosed investments, and targets $1.6 million by age 30.
