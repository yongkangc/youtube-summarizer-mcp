# Coherent Stock Deep Dive: Is COHR a Buy in 2026?

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JOA-2xXC9Fs

## TL;DR

The video frames Coherent (`COHR`) as the more value/restructuring-oriented optical-networking play versus Lumentum. The core thesis: CEO Jim Anderson is narrowing a messy industrial/materials/laser conglomerate toward data-center communications, optical interconnect, silicon carbide, and AI data-center power infrastructure — but the price of that growth is heavy capex and likely negative free cash flow near term.

## Key Insights

- Coherent is now mostly a data-center/communications business.
  - Latest mix cited: roughly **75% data center & communications**, **25% industrial**.
  - Data center/communications revenue grew about **41% YoY**.
  - Pro-forma total revenue growth was about **27%**; reported growth was lower at **21%** because of divestitures.

- CEO Jim Anderson is the restructuring angle.
  - The speaker compares his Coherent playbook to what he did at Lattice Semiconductor: rationalize, rightsize, and focus the company around the highest-return growth areas.
  - Coherent had become unfocused after years of M&A, including the II-VI / Coherent merger.

- Divestitures are part of the value-unlock story.
  - Coherent sold an aerospace/defense business for about **$400M**.
  - It also sold a materials-processing-tools business to Bystronic for about **$51M**; that unit had around **$100M** of annual sales, implying weak or negative profitability.
  - Point: remove margin drag and focus capital on AI/data-center opportunities.

- Gross margin near **40%** matters.
  - The speakers call 40% gross margin a key milestone for an IDM-style manufacturing business.
  - Similar framing to Lumentum: higher margins signal the business is moving toward better product mix and scale.

- Silicon carbide remains strategically important.
  - Coherent has silicon carbide wafer and epi-wafer capabilities.
  - Denso and Mitsubishi Electric each invested about **$500M** in the silicon carbide business, while Coherent retained **75%** equity.
  - The relevance is shifting from auto/industrial toward AI data-center power and grid infrastructure.

- NVIDIA’s **$2B** investment is about scaling supply.
  - Coherent needs cash to build manufacturing capacity for optical circuit switches, co-packaged optics, near-package optics, and related data-center products.
  - NVIDIA needs the products for AI infrastructure buildout.
  - The speaker distinguishes this from warrant-style customer incentives: this is more directly cash for equity to help a supplier scale.

- The main risk is manufacturing economics.
  - Coherent is not a pure software-style growth business; scaling means capex, working capital, and execution risk.
  - Latest quarter free cash flow was cited as meaningfully negative, nearly **-$400M**, due to manufacturing investment.
  - Market may tolerate this while revenue reaccelerates, but cash burn matters.

- Guidance suggests reacceleration.
  - Next-quarter revenue guidance: roughly **$1.9B to just over $2.0B**.
  - Gross margin guidance: around **41%**.
  - Revenue growth could be around **30%+** if the ramp holds.

- Coherent vs Lumentum framing:
  - Lumentum = more momentum optical play.
  - Coherent = more value/restructuring play, with possible future asset sales unlocking value.
  - Both are optical beneficiaries outside the obvious NVIDIA/Broadcom names.

## Investor Read-Through

- Bull case: Coherent becomes a key optical/data-center supply-chain bottleneck, with Jim Anderson focusing the portfolio and NVIDIA helping fund the scale-up.
- Bear case: manufacturing scale-up consumes cash, margins disappoint, and the company remains too complex despite divestitures.
- The setup is not “cheap AI exposure” automatically. It is a capex-heavy supplier bet where the edge is whether optical networking demand proves durable enough to justify the investment cycle.

## One-Line Summary

Coherent is a restructuring + AI optical-networking supplier bet: better focus, strong data-center growth, and NVIDIA-backed capacity expansion, but with real capex/free-cash-flow risk.
