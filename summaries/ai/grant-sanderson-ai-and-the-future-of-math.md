# Grant Sanderson (3Blue1Brown) – AI and the future of math

Source: [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TfyPshgMbug)

Source quality: YouTube captions. Transcript is coherent; names and math terms may have minor auto-caption errors.

## TL;DR

Grant Sanderson argues that AI progress in math is real but spiky. IMO gold does not equal AGI because contest math contains trainable subdomains, and even major theorem proving is only one part of mathematical work. The deeper frontier is whether AI can generate useful conjectures, definitions, explanations, and cross-field connections.

The investor/operator read: math is a clean lab for watching AI move from benchmark-solving toward research automation. Biggest near-term unlock is not one genius model solving everything; it is parallel digital researchers exploring many contexts, refreshing failed approaches, using verifiers, and letting humans judge taste and direction.

## Core claim

Math is one of the fastest-moving AI domains, but it shows why benchmark wins can mislead.

- IMO performance is impressive, but IMO problems are partly trainable.
- Geometry became easier because brute-force solvers work well.
- Combinatorics remains harder because it is more playful and context-breaking.
- A Millennium Prize solution would be a bigger signal, but still may not imply broad economic automation.
- The key question is what kind of solution AI produces: brute force, formal proof, new connection, or new conceptual framework.

## Why IMO gold did not mean AGI

Grant's prior answer aged well: IMO gold becomes another benchmark, not a magic AGI threshold.

Reason:

- Math frontier is spiky even inside math.
- Some subfields are easy to formalize or brute force.
- Others require taste, abstraction, reframing, or escaping misleading context.
- White-collar automation may require different capabilities than solving contest problems.

## Real frontier: conjectures and definitions

Grant thinks theorem proving gets too much credit. In math, high-value work often starts earlier:

- choosing the right definition
- asking the right question
- finding useful abstraction
- making a cross-field connection
- explaining why a proof works

He uses Galois theory as core example. Lagrange did not solve quintics, but asked the symmetry question that later made Galois theory possible. That kind of taste is hard to score with a benchmark.

## Proof vs understanding

Important distinction: proof is not explanation.

Grant cites the idea of an "unsolved expository problem": a theorem may be proven, but humans still do not understand the right explanation for why it is true.

AI may first produce proofs before producing human-legible conceptual understanding. In that world, mathematicians shift from proving to:

- consolidating proofs
- finding higher-level concepts
- explaining why arguments work
- turning proof artifacts into reusable mental models

But Grant also suspects AI may become good at explanation too, because the same ability that creates a useful concept may also help explain it.

## Cross-field connections

They discuss Riemann hypothesis and the Montgomery-Dyson story: number-theory statistics unexpectedly matching random matrix theory from physics.

This is the kind of "lightning bolt" AI may become useful at:

- know many fields deeply
- notice structural analogy
- connect previously separate concepts
- suggest which connection may be worth exploring

Hard part: there is no clean score for "good conjecture" or "good connection." You may only see it through a tone shift among mathematicians using the tool.

## Digital-mind advantage

Biggest AI advantage may not be raw IQ. It is scale and context control.

AI systems can:

- spawn many agents in parallel
- give agents different assumptions or biases
- try proving and disproving same claim
- erase context and restart fresh
- merge or compare attempts later

This matters because humans often get trapped by context. Grant mentions an IMO-style problem where trained solvers missed the simple solution because they approached it like contest math. AI could systematically run multiple framings.

## Autoresearch and verifiers

They discuss Karpathy-style autoresearch: agents modify code, run experiments, and keep changes that improve results.

Math equivalent:

- explore possible axiom systems
- generate candidate structures
- use automated provers/verifiers to check correctness
- use supervisor models to judge whether direction is interesting

Lean/formal systems help because they constrain correctness. Natural-language math may still work if verifier and meta-verifier systems become strong enough.

Grant likes formal exploration because it can go outside human phrasing and prior heuristics, like AlphaGo's move 37.

## Writing and creativity analogy

They compare math research to writing.

LLMs can summarize and explain existing material, but struggle with the thing that makes great writing worth reading:

- choosing an unpredictable but correct move
- building a coherent narrative
- knowing what is interesting
- modeling the reader's mind
- challenging the user's frame instead of flattering it

Grant thinks LLMs currently feel more like Wikipedia than a great authored book: useful, broad, correct-ish, but lacking crafted motivation.

## How to use LLMs for learning

Best use: pair LLM with a strong human-created artifact.

Grant's advice:

- Ask LLMs who or what to read, not just for direct explanations.
- Prefer great teachers/authors over arbitrary topic coverage.
- Use books, lectures, or videos to provide conceptual sequence.
- Use LLMs to clarify local branches around that sequence.

Example: using Steven Strogatz's *Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos* plus lectures plus LLM support. The human author supplies the right order and motivation; LLM helps debug confusion.

## Education implication

LLMs are too sycophantic and too eager to answer the literal question.

Great teachers often say: that is not the right question; organize the concepts this other way.

This requires theory of mind. The model must understand the learner's misconception, not just produce a correct explanation.

## Economic implications

They are cautious on whether pure math breakthroughs directly create huge economic value.

- Some applied math and PDE/dynamics work clearly helps engineering, simulation, CFD, aircraft design, materials, and physical systems.
- Solving famous pure math problems may not instantly unlock industry productivity.
- Over five years, they expect some economically useful spillovers from AI math progress, but likely through applied domains and incremental improvements.
- Awkward possibility: AI math progress may reveal that some pure math has become detached from physical/economic usefulness.

## Investor/operator read-through

- Math is a leading indicator for research automation because verification is cleaner than in most domains.
- Benchmarks understate and overstate progress at same time: solving IMO is less general than it sounds, but agentic parallel search may unlock workflows humans cannot match.
- Strongest near-term systems may be AI research teams, not single AI geniuses.
- Verifiers and supervisor models become key infrastructure.
- Human taste remains scarce: deciding which conjectures, definitions, explanations, and connections matter.
- Education products should combine curated human pedagogy with LLM interactivity, not replace the curriculum with chat.

## Kill tests

- AI proves hard theorems but fails to produce useful abstractions or conjectures.
- Formal proofs scale, but natural-language understanding does not.
- Agent swarms generate huge theorem junk with no taste filter.
- Mathematicians do not show a tone shift from "toy helper" to "research collaborator."
- Economic spillovers from AI math remain weak outside applied simulation, engineering, and materials.

## One-line summary

AI math progress matters less because it wins Olympiads and more because it tests whether digital researchers can generate taste, abstractions, and cross-field connections at scale.
