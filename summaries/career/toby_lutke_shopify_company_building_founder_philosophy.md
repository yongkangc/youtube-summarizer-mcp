## Summary

This is an in-depth conversation with Toby LÃ¼tke, founder and CEO of Shopify, covering his philosophy on company building, product development, and entrepreneurship. Toby shares how he fundamentally reimagined Shopify's operating model during COVID-19 by replacing traditional executives with founder-operators, implementing engineering principles to company structure, and rejecting Silicon Valley orthodoxy. He discusses his belief that companies are social technologies that enable people to pursue ideas full-time, the importance of differentiation over mimicry, and how he approaches everything from compensation systems to office design through first principles thinking. The conversation reveals Toby's contrarian approach to running a $200+ billion company, including going public early, embracing constraints, and treating company building as an engineering problem to be solved iteratively.

## Key Insights

- **Companies as social technology**: Companies are institutions that make it socially acceptable to spend 14+ hours per day pursuing ideas. They're a relatively recent invention (500 years old in modern form) that allow people to work on counterfactuals to the existing world and get market feedback
- **Modern companies are still terrible**: Despite running a $200B+ company, Toby believes all companies today are poorly built. In 20 years, we'll look back embarrassed at current practices, similar to looking at your first podcast episode
- **The COVID pivot saved Shopify**: During COVID, Toby reviewed every single project in the company (taking 16-hour days), canceled 60% of them, and turned over his entire executive team within a year. This forced reckoning revealed widespread "boondoggles" and misalignment
- **Trust falling vs. active leadership**: Before COVID, Toby was "cosplaying" as a traditional CEO, trust-falling and letting teams self-direct. He learned that executives were directing his attention only to projects they wanted him to see, while telling teams what he supposedly wanted based on limited context
- **Founders perform differently in crisis**: During COVID, Toby discovered it was almost impossible to predict who would rise to the occasion. People he expected to excel didn't, and vice versa. The unifying factor: founders of acquired companies consistently performed because they'd been responsible for people's livelihoods
- **The founder channel**: Toby maintains a Slack channel with founders of companies Shopify acquired. During COVID, when struggling, he turned to them for help and ultimately promoted many to executive roles, including taking individual contributors and putting them in charge of large organizations
- **Irritants are valuable**: Founders don't settle. They talk in absolutes. When something is broken, they say so, even if everyone else has agreed to move on. Companies naturally try to isolate these people (like "skunk works" teams), but Toby deliberately put them in leadership
- **Shopify OS - engineering a company**: Toby built a Python-based "desire state system" that models the entire company structure - departments, levels, titles, reporting relationships. It revealed 8,000 people had 5,500 different titles, with inconsistent hierarchies across teams
- **Desire state systems eliminate politics**: When the head of sales requests 50 people, the system automatically shows the tradeoffs - which engineering roles would be lost or what other changes are required. No more backroom deals or vague promises
- **The mastery system**: Shopify extended the engineering concept of individual contributor tracks to all disciplines. You can make as much money and advance as far as a "master" in your craft as you could as a VP, removing the pressure to become a manager
- **Compensation with agency**: After the stock dropped 80%, Toby rebuilt the compensation system so employees choose quarterly how to split their compensation between cash, stock options, RSUs, and "shop cash." They can even lock in stock value for 3 years. This gives employees agency instead of being passive recipients
- **Differentiation even if it's worse**: Toby agrees with James Dyson's principle - make it different even if it's worse initially. Copying creates 7/10 solutions with no mastery. Building from first principles might create a 6/10, but you can iterate to 11/10 because you understand it deeply
- **The SpaceX Raptor evolution**: Toby considers the visual evolution of SpaceX's Raptor rocket engine the most inspiring image in existence - showing how subtraction and iteration create masterpieces through team effort
- **Companies reject excellence by addition**: Very few teams can move forward by subtraction. Most companies accumulate complexity, while true progress comes from removing unnecessary elements
- **Five words change everything**: Toby's co-founder Daniel taught him that adding "for example" before giving feedback transforms defensiveness into collaboration. Small framing changes have massive effects
- **Silicon Valley distance advantage**: Starting in Ottawa meant Toby got aspirational advice from Valley founders (their highlight reel), not the messy reality. This accidentally helped - he was comparing Shopify's average to everyone else's aspirations
- **Translation forces innovation**: Having to translate ideas from Silicon Valley to Ottawa forced Toby to localize and improve them rather than copy directly. Ignorance became a competitive advantage
- **Going public was contrarian**: When Shopify IPO'd in 2015 at $1-1.5B valuation with 800 people, everyone said stay private longer. Toby saw this as an unforced error - public markets need growth, and accredited investor laws just concentrate returns among the wealthy
- **The IPO as differentiation**: Shopify made a documentary-style roadshow video instead of a CEO with a camcorder. They looked for every opportunity within SEC constraints to do things differently. It became a major conversation piece
- **Stock price is just betting**: When Shopify dropped 80%, Toby felt relieved because the 50x revenue valuation was unsustainable. He focused on fundamentals, viewing the public market as a betting market separate from the real business value
- **The Formula 1 mindset**: Formula 1 teams read the rulebook to beat it, not comply with it. Toby applies this to every constraint, from SEC regulations to internal policies - finding creative solutions within the rules
- **Public company as public Shopify**: Their motto was "we are not going public, we are creating a public version of Shopify." They refused to become a different company just because they had public shareholders
- **Books are life cheat codes**: Reading biographies gives access to entire careers in hours. Toby has read 410+ founder biographies and believes if you don't read books you live one lifetime, if you do you live thousands
- **Business books are mostly useless**: Written by people who have time to write (not people building companies), business books tend to reflect the author's bias - if they're a salesperson, every problem is solved by sales
- **Nostalgia is dangerous**: Nostalgia used to be listed on death certificates in the 1800s. Overcoming sunk cost fallacy is essential, and nostalgia prevents moving forward
- **The corporate raider exercise**: Toby regularly imagines he's a corporate raider who bought bankrupt Shopify and is marching in on day one to turn it around. This mental model helps overcome attachment to past decisions
- **Writing hit pieces on yourself**: Toby literally writes critical assessments of past work to overcome pride and identify improvement areas. This affirmation technique rewrites neural pathways
- **Affirmations actually work**: Despite being logical/analytical, Toby uses affirmations extensively. He overcame his fear of public speaking by writing "I love public speaking" for 10 minutes daily for a week
- **Messages in a bottle**: Toby writes scheduled messages to his future self about insights, process improvements, and topics to revisit, giving himself space for repetition and reflection
- **The brain is a narrative alignment mechanism**: The brain isn't good at record-keeping; it reconciles history with your current identity. You can exploit this by changing your identity through affirmations

## Main Arguments or Thesis

**Core Thesis**: Building companies is an engineering problem that should be approached through first principles thinking rather than mimicking existing practices. Most companies are poorly designed due to path dependence and orthodoxy. The best approach is to make the company itself a technology - a deliberately engineered system that enables creative people to do their best work.

**Supporting Arguments**:

1. **Companies are technologies, not natural forms**: The modern company is only 500 years old and evolved from quasi-governmental entities. We shouldn't treat current practices as inevitable or optimal

2. **Differentiation demands new ideas**: You cannot build something better by copying what exists. Even if your different approach is initially worse, you have mastery over it and can iterate to excellence

3. **Context over credentials**: The person with the most context should make decisions, not the most senior person. This applies from code-level technical decisions to company-wide strategic choices

4. **Environment shapes behavior**: Rather than posting policies about how people should behave, design the environment so the correct behavior is intuitive and natural

5. **Founders have unique value**: People who have been responsible for others' livelihoods develop capabilities that can't be replicated through training. This changes you fundamentally

6. **Quantification kills excellence**: The things that matter most for great companies (taste, quality, craft) cannot be reduced to numbers or lists. Attempting to do so attracts performative compliance

7. **Agency over prescription**: Giving people agency (like choosing their own compensation mix) creates ownership and eliminates resentment better than any prescribed "fair" system

8. **AI accelerates everything**: The rate of change is now measured in weeks. Organizations and individuals that can't adapt this quickly will be left behind catastrophically

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Use the corporate raider mental model**: Regularly imagine you're a hostile acquirer who bought your bankrupt company. What would you change on day one? This overcomes sunk cost fallacy

2. **Write hit pieces on your own work**: Literally document everything wrong with things you're proud of. This affirmation technique helps you overcome attachment to past decisions

3. **Add "for example" before feedback**: Five words at the start of feedback transforms defensive reaction into collaborative exploration. Frame suggestions as possibilities rather than corrections

4. **Implement desire state systems**: Model what your organization should look like as code/configuration, then use it to show tradeoffs. When sales wants 50 people, system shows what engineering roles you'd lose

5. **Create five-person teams**: Military and tech both converge on 5 as optimal team size. Design physical pods that fit 5 comfortably, 7 uncomfortably - eliminates need for team size policies

6. **Phase-based project structure**: Prototype phase (explore), transition review (with AI preview), build phase (full autonomy). Trade accountability for autonomy at phase transitions

7. **Give employees compensation agency**: Let people quarterly adjust their mix of cash/stock/RSUs. Eliminates resentment about stock price changes because they have control

8. **Design environment to make correct behavior intuitive**: Don't post policies about behavior. Make hallways echoey so talking there feels uncomfortable. Put coffee on opposite side from desks to create serendipity

9. **Hire for spiky high-agency**: Look for life stories showing entrepreneurial responses to adversity, not well-rounded credentials. Ask "something went wrong - give me minute by minute how you reacted"

10. **Extend mastery tracks to all disciplines**: Don't force people to become managers to advance. Let people progress in craft excellence with equivalent compensation to VP/leadership tracks

11. **Source everything from your customers**: Shopify sources 100% of office materials from Shopify stores. If something doesn't exist, help someone start that business

12. **Go public earlier than advice suggests**: Accredited investor laws concentrate returns among wealthy. Public markets need growth. Don't spend years figuring out how to stay private

13. **Read rulebooks to beat them**: Approach every constraint like Formula 1 engineers - find creative solutions within rules rather than just complying

14. **Use affirmations systematically**: Spend 10 minutes daily writing the identity you want (e.g., "I love public speaking"). It rewires your prefrontal cortex in about a week

15. **Schedule messages to future self**: Write yourself messages about insights, process improvements, topics to revisit. Give yourself space for repetition over time

16. **Reflexively try AI first**: Make it organizational expectation to attempt AI solution before reverting to traditional methods. What seems obvious today seemed crazy 3 months ago

17. **Create internal founder networks**: Maintain special channels/groups with founders of acquired companies or entrepreneurial employees. Turn to them in crisis

18. **Replace Norman doors immediately**: Any affordance that makes people predictably do the wrong thing should be fixed immediately. It represents fundamental design failure

19. **Connect all work to mission**: Everyone should be able to draw line from their daily tasks to company mission. Support isn't helping people - it's helping people not give up on entrepreneurship

20. **Meet teams at their abstraction level**: Good executives translate between frames of reference - helping tactical teams understand how their work connects to strategic goals

21. **Eliminate titles and level inconsistency**: Use software to model organization structure. Shopify had 5,500 titles for 8,000 people with inconsistent hierarchies across teams

22. **Make space for creative exploration**: Define the problem space (the "box"), equip the right team, then give full autonomy. Best solutions come from exploration, not prescription

23. **Separate quantifiable from bets**: At infrastructure level, measure everything. At strategic level (market entry, AI trends), work with trend lines and place informed bets

24. **Build company you'd want to work for**: Dystopian outcome is spending 10 years building another company you wouldn't want to work at. Why would you use your life for that?

25. **Geographic consensus strategy**: Instead of competing in saturated markets, create consensus in new geography that you're the best option. Toby couldn't beat Google pay in Valley but dominated Eastern Canada
