# All-In Podcast: Socialists Sweep NYC, China Catches Up in Coding, AI Memory Crunch, Micron's Blowout Quarter

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w8ah_tA0yfg

Published: 2026-06-27

Focus: stock-market / alpha read-through only.

## TL;DR

The investable part of this episode is Gavin Baker’s AI-infra bottleneck framework. His main claim: DRAM/HBM is the real AI bottleneck, Micron/SK Hynix/Samsung have structurally better pricing power than old DRAM cycles, and the next equity-alpha question shifts from “who has AI demand?” to “who can secure memory, power, cooling, and megawatts fastest?”

## Main market points

- **Micron / HBM call was the cleanest public-equity alpha.** Jason says Micron revenue went from about **$9B to $42B**, beat expectations by **16%**, guided Q4 around **$50B vs $43B**, and stock was up massively since Gavin’s earlier HBM-maker call.

- **Gavin: DRAM is the bottleneck that matters.** He pushes back on “bottleneck bros” chasing obscure components. His view: memory capacity + bandwidth are foundational to every AI model, so DRAM/HBM matters more than lasers, capacitors, power semiconductors, NAND, or HDDs.

- **HBM supply is structurally tight.** Only **Micron, SK Hynix, Samsung** can make the AI-server-grade HBM/DRAM at scale. China’s CXMT may flood consumer DRAM, but Gavin says that does not solve high-end AI-server DRAM.

- **DRAM business model may be changing.** Micron has supply agreements with floor/ceiling pricing. Gavin says these cover roughly **50% of revenue with four customers**, and floor pricing is above prior-cycle peak gross margins. Translation: less commodity-cycle chaos, more contracted scarcity rent.

- **Cross-sectional valuation angle:** Gavin says memory stocks still look cheap versus rest of AI. Wafer-fab equipment and other semi supply-chain names have already rerated; DRAM may not fully reflect improved industry structure.

- **AI capex mix shifting toward memory.** Gavin estimates DRAM could be **30–40% of hyperscaler capex next year**. If right, the bottleneck rents accrue to memory makers and anything that expands usable AI capacity.

- **Consumer-electronics inflation is side effect.** Apple and console price hikes are framed as AI data centers “hoovering up” DRAM. AI demand is price-insensitive; consumer-device demand is more elastic.

- **Buildability becomes alpha.** Gavin/Jason/Chamath discuss Micron’s New York fab getting slowed by environmental/regulatory issues. Alpha lens: states/countries that can permit and build fabs/data centers faster become advantaged.

- **Data-center cost inflation is huge.** Gavin frames a 1GW data center as roughly **$35B in Nvidia semiconductors + $25B in power/cooling equipment**. If ground-based build costs rise, scarce powered land becomes “diamond” asset.

- **Orbital compute thesis:** Gavin says if Starship launch cost can fall to about **$5B per gigawatt**, space compute might be **$40B** versus **$60B+** terrestrial. Speculative but shows how power/cooling/entitlement inflation changes compute economics.

- **Tesla/SpaceX/Megapod speculation:** Trademark filing suggests modular AI compute hardware. Chamath/others discuss Tesla supercharger/power assets and modular compute pods. Investable angle is not “Tesla definitely wins”; it is that modular, prefabricated, powered compute could compress build cycles.

- **Distributed inference > distributed training.** Travis/Gavin say distributed training suffers badly when GPUs are physically separated. But inference can be disaggregated into **prefill** and **decode**, creating room for distributed inference clouds and longer useful lives for H100/A100/H200-class GPUs.

- **Old GPUs may keep value longer.** Gavin says pairing older Nvidia GPUs with decode-optimized chips like Groq/Cerebras could make competitive inference systems. That lowers financing risk and extends asset life.

- **IPO / supply overhang nuance.** Gavin argues SpaceX IPO overhang may be overstated because employees/investors had semiannual liquidity for years and many employees bought at IPO. He warns against simplistic “short lockup expiration” trades.

- **Cerebras IPO lesson:** If a stock breaks deal price, some PMs sell mechanically; shorts know this and can push it through. Gavin’s operator advice: price IPOs so they do not break deal price in first 9 months.

- **Cerebras actual KPI:** not generic AI demand, but how quickly it can bring on power. Gavin says if Cerebras added **50MW/month in 2027**, rough math could imply around **$9B cloud compute run-rate** exiting that year, but execution is very hard.

- **Anthropic valuation shock:** Gavin says Anthropic could trade around **$3T** public, based on massive projected revenue scale and inference gross margins. Key point is not exact number; public markets can absorb large offerings because private value is moving public, not appearing from nowhere.

## Alpha map

- **Best clean public-equity bucket:** memory/HBM scarcity — Micron, SK Hynix, Samsung; watch whether contract floors protect margins through cycle.
- **Second bucket:** AI power/cooling/buildout — companies tied to megawatts, cooling, switchgear, modular data-center assembly, permitting speed.
- **Third bucket:** inference infrastructure — old GPU reuse, prefill/decode split, distributed inference, Groq/Cerebras-style decode acceleration.
- **Private/public crossover:** SpaceX, xAI/SpaceX compute, Anthropic, Cerebras. Main tradable edge likely IPO mechanics, power-onboarding KPIs, and lockup/supply misunderstanding.

## My read

Real alpha is not “AI demand strong.” Everyone knows that. Better question: which scarce input is still underpriced versus forward AI capex? Gavin says answer is memory first, then powered compute. I buy the framework; I would still sanity-check memory names against cycle history, customer concentration, contract durability, and whether HBM margins get competed away once capacity lands.

## One-line summary

All-In’s market alpha: AI trade is rotating from model hype to bottleneck underwriting — DRAM/HBM, megawatts, cooling, modular compute, and IPO supply mechanics.
