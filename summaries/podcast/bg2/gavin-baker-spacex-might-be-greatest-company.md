# Gavin Baker: SpaceX Might Be the Greatest Company of All Time

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PW5n3ZnEJN0

Guest: Gavin Baker

## TL;DR

Gavin Baker’s core read: public markets can tolerate huge investment cycles when the asset is exceptional, and SpaceX may be one of the rare companies with enough execution speed, physical bottleneck control, and demand pull to justify that patience. The most investable idea is not just rockets: it is SpaceX/xAI as a vertically integrated AI infrastructure platform — terrestrial compute now, possible orbital compute later, with retail demand and AI capex creating unusual supply/demand dynamics.

## Key points

- IPO execution looked clean. Baker thought Goldman and Morgan Stanley handled the IPO well: a controlled ~20% pop rather than a chaotic melt-up/melt-down. He argues the market will care about quarters, but still has more long-duration tolerance than many private-market investors assume.

- Public markets can fund long investment cycles. He compares SpaceX to Tesla and Amazon/AWS: every quarter matters, but if investors believe the reinvestment creates a much larger asset, they can tolerate heavy capex and delayed profitability.

- Near-term SpaceX variable #1: how fast it can energize terrestrial compute. Baker says the key question is how quickly SpaceX can bring gigawatts online. He cites the idea that SpaceX monetizes gigawatts at a much higher rate than average neoclouds, with one Google deal discussed around **$50B per gigawatt**.

- Near-term SpaceX variable #2: how much compute is training vs inference. Labs constantly face the allocation tradeoff. Baker reads SpaceX’s large Rubin order as a sign it is confident it can bring data centers online quickly.

- “Token factory” may be enough. Asked whether SpaceX needs to become a full AWS-style cloud, Baker says being a token factory is likely more than enough for the next **5–10 years**. If the company eventually needs storage, databases, and cloud primitives, he thinks it can build them later.

- Meta may have hidden optionality. Baker is increasingly looking at **EV / net PP&E** as a metric: installed “atoms on earth” with low obsolescence may appreciate in value. Meta’s multiple suggests skepticism about its ability to monetize the asset base, but he thinks Zuckerberg can pivot if the stock/AI talent market forces it.

- Big-cap behavior is partly stock-price driven. AI is existential, but the stock price still matters because employee grants and talent retention matter. If the stock is weak, retaining “10,000x engineers” gets harder.

- Retail is a real force. Baker says retail has been more powerful over the last **3.5 years** than at any other point in his career, including 2000. For SpaceX specifically, lockup analysis may overstate supply because employees and private investors already had semiannual liquidity windows for years.

- “Token path” is the right lens, but not every AI-adjacent asset qualifies. CDNs like Cloudflare have a path into the token path and are already delivering some low-latency tokens, but Baker estimates they currently deliver less than **1%** of global token volume, maybe less than **10 bps**. The premium is for latency where latency matters, especially voice.

- Bottleneck trade may be shifting. Last year’s game was finding the next bottleneck. Baker says the next game is identifying what has enduring franchise value after bottlenecks normalize.

- Orbital compute is economically plausible if Starship is reusable. Baker’s rough math: terrestrial gigawatt compute may cost around **$60B/GW**, including roughly **$25B** for power/cooling. If reusable Starship lowers launch cost to around **$5B/GW**, orbital compute could be around **$30B/GW** all-in versus **$60B/GW** on earth. Big caveat: this depends on Starship reuse actually working at scale.

- Sovereign AI likely becomes localized fine-tuning, not frontier competition. Baker thinks most countries will want sovereign AI for defense/intelligence/culture, but outside the US and China it probably means taking a strong open model, doing RL/SFT on local language/values, running it in local data centers, and calling that sovereign AI. He does not see most countries competing at the frontier.

- China may fall further behind. His view: frontier AI requires not just chips and power, but talent, usage, and feedback loops. Without those, sovereign frontier AI is hard.

- SpaceX may be a once-in-a-career company. Baker says SpaceX could be one of the most important/iconic companies ever, maybe the most. He compares its personal significance to owning Nvidia and Tesla extremely early, but says rocket launches are uniquely visceral.

- He pushes back on “trillionaire villain” framing. His argument: Elon/SpaceX/Tesla have decarbonized transport, connected low-income areas to internet, enabled hospitals/schools, and may solve parts of data-center environmental constraints through space infrastructure.

- There are still many sub-$2B opportunities, mostly private. Baker says AI knock-on effects in bio, materials science, Neuralink-like companies, and SaaS still create many venture-scale opportunities. Public small-cap land was dull until AI bottleneck trades appeared.

- Warning on AI small-cap pump dynamics. He flags shady behavior where large social accounts can post a thesis on a tiny ~$35M market-cap company and send it up massively. Unlike sell-side research, anonymous social promotion lacks compliance infrastructure.

## Investor read

- Theory of edge discussed: own bottleneck/franchise assets where AI demand growth structurally exceeds market expectations, especially assets that can convert physical infrastructure into high-value tokens faster than peers.

- Best bull case: SpaceX becomes a vertically integrated AI infrastructure company: launch economics + Starlink distribution + terrestrial compute execution + xAI/token demand + potential orbital compute.

- Main underwriting questions:
  - Can SpaceX keep bringing gigawatts online faster than everyone else?
  - Are $/GW monetization rates durable or temporarily inflated by scarcity?
  - Does token demand compound for 5–10 years, or does pricing collapse?
  - Does Starship reuse make orbital compute actually cheaper, not just conceptually elegant?
  - How much of this is already priced after IPO?

- My read: this is not a simple “buy rockets” thesis. It is an AI-infra bottleneck thesis with SpaceX as the one company that might own multiple bottlenecks at once: launch, power/site execution, network distribution, and model-side demand.

## One-line summary

Gavin Baker sees SpaceX as a potential once-in-a-career AI infrastructure compounder: not just rockets, but a token factory with launch, compute, distribution, and possibly orbital data-center advantages.
