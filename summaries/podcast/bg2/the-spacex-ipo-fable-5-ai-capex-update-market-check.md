# The SpaceX IPO, Fable 5, AI Capex Update & Market Check

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Tx9jT2c6e3U

Guests: Gavin Baker, Andrew Fox, Clark Tang

## TL;DR

Latest BG2 episode is basically a bull case for SpaceX/X/xAI as a combined space + AI infrastructure platform, plus a check-in on frontier models, AI capex, Nvidia, and the market. Their view: consensus still underestimates how much compute demand long-running AI agents will create, and SpaceX may be one of the few assets that compounds across launch, Starlink/direct-to-cell, terrestrial AI compute, xAI models, and eventually orbital compute.

## Key points

- SpaceX IPO framing: reported IPO price around $135/share and valuation around $1.77T, with bank forecasts around $160B 2028 revenue. Bears see an extreme jump from roughly $18B last year to $160B in three years; bulls say it becomes plausible if you break the business into components.

- Three big SpaceX levers:
  - launch/reusability as the crown jewel
  - Starlink/direct-to-cell communications
  - AI compute/xAI, including terrestrial data centers and possibly future orbital compute

- Launch economics are the foundation. Rapid Starship reusability is the unlock: Falcon launch cost is discussed around $1,500/kg, Starship potentially around $250/kg and eventually lower as reuse improves.

- Starlink still has low penetration. They argue global Starlink broadband penetration is under 1%, with potential for hundreds of millions of terminals/users if Starship rapidly reusable launch works and competition stays limited.

- Direct-to-cell is treated as a major revenue driver. They frame it as “better, faster, cheaper” telecom, with one estimate around $50B revenue equating to only ~0.3% penetration of global telecom.

- “Elon Web Services”: SpaceX/xAI is framed as building AWS-like AI compute under everyone’s nose. They point to deals with Google and Anthropic, high operating profit per gigawatt, and a cited estimate of ~55% IRR on Colossus 1.

- SpaceX’s AI compute edge is speed + supplier credibility. They argue Elon can secure power, land, turbines/generators, financing, and GPU supply faster than startup neoclouds because suppliers make money faster when GPUs are energized faster.

- Orbital compute is the long-term call option, not necessary for IPO valuation. If Starship can launch reusable payload cheaply, space-based data centers could avoid some terrestrial bottlenecks: land, cooling, power, permitting.

- Their orbital compute math: around 5MW capacity per Starship launch, and roughly $5B/GW capex to put compute in space before accounting for failed GPUs/satellites. They compare that with terrestrial data center non-GPU infrastructure around $20-25B/GW, or about $60B/GW total including GPUs/silicon.

- Cursor/xAI matters because coding may be the fastest path to AGI/ASI. They say Cursor and Anthropic may have more proprietary coding-token data than exists on the public internet. Cursor-trained models improved quickly, and if xAI integrates Cursor data into pretraining, xAI could become a serious coding-model player.

- Model frontier view: Anthropic looks strong after Fable 5/Mythos and revenue traction. They also view xAI/Grok 4.3 as close to frontier, especially if Cursor data is integrated into training.

- Long-running agents change the benchmark. Snapshot benchmarks matter less; time/tokens/test-time compute matter more. If models can think/work for hours or days, many hard tasks become economically useful.

- Open-source models may lag but remain important. They discuss Jensen’s model-routing view: frontier models capture value now because they can handle long-running tasks, but open source may catch up with a lag for everyday tasks.

- Nvidia remains stronger than expected. A year ago many expected custom silicon/Broadcom/TPUs to take more share; instead Nvidia continues out-executing and demand appears to be accelerating.

- AI capex math: Morgan Stanley’s 2027 capex forecast rose from ~$950B to ~$1.1T, but they think true number may be closer to ~$1.5T excluding some areas like SpaceX/CoreWeave. The concern is whether inference revenue can support this.

- Their answer: yes, probably. They cite AI lab revenue around ~$300B in 2027, possibly low, with gross margins maybe 50-70%. They think inference revenue could end this year well above $200B, so the capex math is starting to work.

- Market check: despite war/Iran risk, ~$100 oil, CPI above 4%, internet down ~15%, software down ~8%, AI-linked equities have held up because market underestimated AI revenue and compute demand.

## Investor read

- Their core bet: AI demand is bigger/faster than consensus, and SpaceX is a rare asset levered to both physical bottlenecks and model/software upside.
- The real thesis is not “SpaceX is a rocket company.” It is “SpaceX/xAI becomes a vertically integrated compute-and-connectivity platform with launch cost advantages, power/site execution, satellite distribution, and model/data advantages.”
- Main risks: Starship rapid reusability slips, direct-to-cell economics disappoint, AI capex/revenue math rolls over, xAI fails to stay near frontier, or IPO valuation already capitalizes too much perfection.

## One-line summary

BG2’s latest episode argues SpaceX is becoming the cleanest public-market proxy for the AI compute supercycle plus space infrastructure, with Starship reusability as the key physical unlock and xAI/Cursor as the model-layer upside.
