# Gavin Baker on Investing, AI, and Resilience

📺 Source: https://x.com/i/status/2074886999945965693

## TL;DR

Gavin Baker frames investing as a game of skill + chance where edge comes from matching history with current facts to form a differentiated view. The best parts are not stock tips but process: know your emotional makeup, survive drawdowns, learn from being wrong early, and build conviction around physical bottlenecks like watts, wafers, and exceptional founders.

## Key Insights

- **Investing = history + current reality + differentiated future view** — Baker says investing clicked because it felt like a game of skill and chance where the way to win is combining deep history with an accurate read of the present to forecast what happens next.
- **Fidelity was formative because it exposed him to many winning styles** — his point: there is no single correct investment philosophy. You need a process that fits your emotional makeup so you can stay rational under pressure.
- **Early semiconductor win came from simple inventory analysis** — at 23, he wrote a bearish semi note before the dot-com crash after seeing customer inventories, finished goods, and semiconductor inventories all at highs while stocks had extreme multiples.
- **Jensen and Hock shaped his view of exceptional operators** — he met Jensen Huang early and now calls him one of the most exceptional people he has met. On Hock Tan: Broadcom model is to find drained profit pools with little competition left, then price up.
- **Large-cap pharma blowup taught humility** — after early success in semis, he moved to pharma at 25, got the sector call wrong, and went from star analyst to bottom-ranked. He says that kind of humbling early is one of the best things that can happen to a young investor.
- **Public equities are the hardest competitive arena** — unlike PE/VC where you compete with a handful of bidders, public equities mean competing with everyone on earth, including smart people who do not look polished.
- **You cannot succeed if you do not love the game** — investing pain is unavoidable. Love of the game is what keeps you going through drawdowns.
- **Bubbles usually follow revolutionary technologies** — he cites Carlotta Perez: markets correctly identify revolutionary tech, opinion diversity collapses, bubble forms, overbuild happens, then crash.
- **AI may avoid a classic bubble because of constraints** — Baker says current tech valuations are not bubble-like; tech multiples have compressed and are not extreme. AI capex risk exists, but **watts and wafers** plus internet-bubble scars may prevent true overbuild.
- **AI ROI is temporarily noisy** — Blackwell spend is huge and training-heavy, so near-term ROI can look weak. But he thinks agentic AI is already real and Blackwell ROI should become clear through coding/agent workloads.
- **Tesla lesson: first-principles superiority matters** — he recalls a small Tesla meeting with Elon when Tesla was around **$1.5B market cap**. EVs made sense to him because batteries were deflationary, center of gravity lower, traction control better, safety structurally better, and storage/noise advantages obvious.
- **Stay close to exceptional companies forever** — he says he has read essentially every Tesla and Nvidia transcript. When you find an exceptional company, keep following it even when not formally covering it.
- **Elon’s edge = mission attracts elite engineers** — Tesla, SpaceX, X, and xAI give exceptional engineers a higher-mission alternative to optimizing ad clicks. Baker thinks this talent magnet is underexplained.
- **AI and creativity: answer with humility** — he says maybe nothing human is irreplaceable, but human brains remain extremely energy-efficient at ~20–30 watts. In a power-constrained world, humans likely retain value for a long time.
- **Video game platforms may benefit more than game studios** — AI world models could cut AAA game development cost by ~90%, creating content explosion. That may hurt incumbent studios via competition but help platforms.
- **Human IO is bottleneck** — Neuralink matters if it lets humans absorb and output information faster than speech/writing/typing.
- **Drawdowns require systems** — he uses exercise, books, old journals, and prior drawdown notes to stay centered. Goal: decision quality improves during stress rather than collapsing.
- **Being early = being wrong** — if you are losing money, you are wrong. Do not blame market stupidity; ask what decision to make next.
- **Know whether you panic early or double down late** — mentor line: “You either panic early or double down late, and almost no one does both.” Baker says he is not a panic-early person; he is a double-down-late person, so knowing that shapes his drawdown process.
- **Advice to young people: be kind and be scrappy** — many are one or the other; the rare combo compounds through reputation and reciprocal networks.
- **Reputation starts earlier than you think** — people remember how you behaved in your 20s more than whether you were top-ranked in an analyst class.

## Investor / Operator Read-Through

- **Theory of edge:** Baker’s edge is not one secret model; it is long-duration curiosity, physical-supply-chain understanding, founder/operator underwriting, and emotional fit with his own process.
- **AI thesis:** strongest practical frame is `watts + wafers + scars`. Power and TSMC capacity constrain overbuild; internet-bubble trauma constrains valuation; agentic AI creates real demand.
- **Process takeaway:** in drawdown, first admit you are wrong. Then decide whether your process says cut, hedge, or double down. Do not let ego decide.
- **YK-relevant line:** one-man hedge fund process should be designed around personal temperament, not imported Buffett/Graham cosplay.

## One-Line Summary

Gavin Baker’s investing lesson: find exceptional companies early, but build the emotional machinery to survive being wrong, because public markets punish ego before they reward insight.
