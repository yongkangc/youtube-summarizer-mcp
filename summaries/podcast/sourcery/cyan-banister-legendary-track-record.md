## Investment Philosophy

- **Humans are “why machines.”** Banister starts each day with a question and follows it through ordinary systems. Asking why taxis fail in rain, why drivers rush, or why gamers charter helicopters can reveal unmet demand before a market category exists.
- **Behavior beats pitch decks.** She became interested in Niantic after watching Ingress players rent helicopters, charter boats, and cross deserts to claim virtual territory. Extreme voluntary behavior was evidence of latent product power.
- **Immerse before investing.** She played Ingress, understood its mechanics, saw how users generated mapping data for Google, then pursued Niantic immediately when it spun out. Her operating knowledge and network value won access even though Niantic did not need her money.
- **Pre-thinking compresses decisions.** Years of questioning taxi economics made Uber legible immediately. Investment speed came from prior curiosity, not impulsiveness.
- **Intuition is compressed observation.** Her “feelings” about investments come from noticing behavior, anomalies, eye movements, incentives, and system details that others ignore. She treats introspection and attention as trainable perception, not mysticism.
- **Do not chase today's hottest deal.** Seed alpha sits in what current infrastructure enables next, where prices remain low and ownership can be meaningful. Hypercompetitive consensus deals usually offer worse economics.
- **Losses calibrate pattern recognition.** Early-stage investors need failed checks to learn attachment, founder treatment, risk tolerance, and which patterns actually matter.
