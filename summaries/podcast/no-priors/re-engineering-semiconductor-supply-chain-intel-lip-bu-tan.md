# No Priors — Re-engineering the Semiconductor Supply Chain with Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=asCgCv2XB4s

Guest: Lip-Bu Tan, CEO of Intel; former CEO of Cadence; founder/investor at Walden

## TL;DR

Lip-Bu Tan frames Intel as both a turnaround and a national/semi-supply-chain infrastructure bet. His plan is: strengthen the balance sheet, simplify products, listen to customers, rebuild engineering accountability, revive leadership CPUs, and make Intel Foundry trusted on yield, defect density, cycle time, IP, packaging, and full-stack systems.

The investor takeaway: semiconductor edge is shifting back to hard bottlenecks — CPU orchestration for agentic AI, memory, power, advanced packaging, interconnect/optics, materials, EDA, and domestic/sovereign manufacturing capacity. Tan’s style is venture-capital at huge scale: identify bottlenecks, back strong teams, partner with hyperscalers/governments/sovereigns, and aim for 10x even from a giant base.

## Key points

- **Why take Intel CEO job:** Tan says Intel is iconic, important to the semiconductor ecosystem, and important to the United States. He frames the job as “one more” mission after Cadence.

- **Turnaround operating principles:** crawl → walk → run. First be humble, listen to customers, simplify product lines, make faster decisions, drive accountability, and have engineering report directly to him so he can understand what went wrong.

- **Balance sheet first:** he highlights US government support, Nvidia/Jensen Huang’s investment, and SoftBank/Masayoshi Son as helping strengthen Intel’s balance sheet.

- **CPU demand is coming back with AI:** he says agentic AI/inference is increasing CPU relevance. Training ratios may move from roughly 1 CPU : 8 GPUs toward 1:4 or even tighter. In reinforcement learning and agent orchestration, developers told him CPU speed/coordination matters.

- **Intel Foundry is a trust/service business:** customers care about yield, defect density, cycle time, reliability, IP availability, and whether missed yields cause revenue misses. Foundry is not just manufacturing; it is service + trust.

- **Full-stack requirement:** Tan says customers increasingly ask for the whole rack, not just silicon. Intel needs silicon, software, packaging, systems, and workload-specific solutions.

- **Tesla/TeraFab collaboration:** Elon Musk wants his own fab. Intel is collaborating to help Tesla move faster to production using Intel technology/processes. Tan likes Musk’s unconventional questioning of every process step.

- **AI reshapes global supply chains:** countries need to play to strengths: cheap energy for data centers, model-training capability in a few countries, manufacturing/talent hubs elsewhere. AI may hit outsourcing-heavy countries first through customer support/IT cuts.

- **AI bottlenecks:** power constraints, helium exposure, memory shortage, CPU/GPU capacity, and semiconductor pricing pressure. Fabs take years to add capacity.

- **Domestic manufacturing matters:** Tan considered exiting foundry but decided doubling down matters for the US and the industry. Supply-chain resilience requires more than dependence on one geography.

- **Respect for TSMC, but need more capacity:** Intel and TSMC can both be needed. Tan repeatedly says he respects TSMC and sees the industry needing more capacity overall.

- **Moore’s Law continues but gets harder:** Intel is moving 18A → 14A and Tan sees paths to 10A and 7A, but costs/difficulty rise. Progress requires partners, equipment vendors, new materials, and packaging.

- **Advanced packaging is a bottleneck:** he highlights TSMC CoWoS and Intel’s next-gen packaging, plus glass substrates, artificial diamond, and new materials.

- **New materials matter:** he names gallium nitride, silicon carbide, indium phosphide, glass, and artificial diamond as areas he has invested in or is watching.

- **Semis are investable again:** Tan remembers when VCs avoided semiconductors. Now Nvidia, Broadcom, TSMC, AMD, Intel, and AI demand have made the sector essential and hot.

- **His semi-investing formula:** look for the bottleneck, what problem is being solved, where talent is, who the customer is, and whether hyperscalers are willing to pay millions / provide warrants because the pain is real.

- **Example bottlenecks:** interconnect, optical signaling, EDA/design automation, power management, thermal, materials, advanced packaging.

- **Talent geography:** US/Silicon Valley/Austin and Israel stand out. Tan praises Israeli founders’ resilience even during war.

- **Physical AI is the next frontier:** beyond agentic AI, he sees physical AI/robotics as a major opportunity, especially with open-source frontier tech.

- **EDA and chip design will be AI-transformed:** Cadence/Synopsys are embracing agentic AI, and startups can disrupt pieces of design/test/materials/manufacturing before being acquired or going public.

- **Founder traits:** nine of ten companies he invested in changed business plans halfway through. He wants teams, not solo founders; open-mindedness; coachability; and ability to form their own plan from feedback.

- **Winning companies in 10 years:** laser-focus on a niche, find the right partners, scale, and increasingly deliver full-stack solutions. Nvidia is his example of a platform company built around CUDA + ecosystem.

- **Industrial policy/capital:** capital-intensive infrastructure needs government funding, sovereign funds, big infrastructure funds, and long-term public investors. VC rounds and valuations in AI infra have become unusually large.

- **What investors miss about Intel:** Tan says investors may underestimate the product upside in PC/client, edge, agentic AI, physical AI, and the long-term potential of foundry once trust blocks are rebuilt. He thinks foundry progress may surface around 2030–2032.

- **10x ambition:** despite Intel’s large base, he says his venture instinct is to seek 10x over 5–10 years. He compares this to Cadence, where shareholder return was roughly 76x–85x over his tenure depending on measurement.

- **Data-center buildout still constrained by supply:** he sees AI infrastructure demand continuing; any slowdown is more likely supply constraint than demand exhaustion. But winners depend on applications, not just raw buildout.

- **Edge/client compute comes back:** robotics, defense, home robots, and physical-world applications may need on-device/client/edge compute rather than only centralized data centers.

## Investor read

- Theory of edge in this episode: **bottleneck investing**. The market pays you if you identify scarce, hard-to-build semiconductor constraints before they become consensus earnings pools.

- Best bottleneck list from Tan:
  - CPU orchestration for agentic AI / inference
  - memory
  - power and thermal
  - interconnect / optical / photonics
  - advanced packaging
  - new substrates/materials
  - EDA/design automation
  - domestic foundry capacity
  - full-stack rack/system solutions

- Intel thesis from Tan:
  - Fix balance sheet.
  - Fix culture/accountability.
  - Rebuild product leadership.
  - Make foundry trusted, not just technically possible.
  - Use AI demand + agentic/physical AI to re-enter growth markets.
  - Move from legacy spreadsheet company to AI-enabled full-stack semi platform.

- Key risk: foundry is brutally hard because customer trust depends on operational proof: yield, cycle time, defects, IP, ecosystem, and reliability. This is not fixed by narrative.

- Key opportunity: if AI pushes compute from centralized training into inference/agents/edge/robotics, CPUs, packaging, power, and full-stack systems become more strategically valuable.

## One-line summary

Lip-Bu Tan’s Intel plan is a venture-style semiconductor turnaround: rebuild trust and product execution, then attack AI-era bottlenecks in CPUs, foundry, packaging, interconnect, power, materials, and full-stack systems.
