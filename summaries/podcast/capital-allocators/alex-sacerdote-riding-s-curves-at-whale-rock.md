# Alex Sacerdote – Riding S-Curves at Whale Rock

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qe4MawRkrxs

## TL;DR

Alex Sacerdote of Whale Rock lays out a tech-investing framework: find a real S-curve, identify the company with durable competitive advantage, then underwrite the earnings power 3–5 years out before the market does. His current read: AI infrastructure is still early, Mag 7 concentration is more digital-platform economics than bubble, and stablecoins look like a real crypto S-curve while much of earlier blockchain was too early / problem-looking-for-solution.

## Key insights

- **Whale Rock framework = S-curve + competitive advantage + underappreciated earnings power.** The edge is not “tech grows”; it is catching the point where barriers to adoption fall and growth becomes rapid but still under-modeled.

- **S-curves make tech more predictable than it looks.** Smartphones went from niche to mainstream once Apple solved price, usability, and network access. That allowed a 1% to 50% adoption move in a few years, creating predictable unit growth.

- **The market often misprices exponential earnings.** Investors anchor on year-one or year-two P/E. Sacerdote wants to estimate year-three to year-five earnings when S-curve growth plus margin expansion can make an “expensive” stock look cheap in hindsight.

- **Examples: Tesla and Nvidia.** Whale Rock bought Tesla around the 2019–2020 EV inflection and later judged it had effectively paid ~4x earnings three years out. It also “loaded up” on Nvidia after ChatGPT in Jan 2023, again underwriting future earnings power rather than near-term P/E.

- **Cloud was a dynamic S-curve.** Early AWS was not just “servers in warehouses”; it absorbed compute, networking, storage, database, and software layers. Whale Rock initially sized the opportunity around $300B after assuming deflation versus a ~$600B traditional IT spend base, then realized it could be closer to the full $600B.

- **Research process is industrial-scale.** Whale Rock’s “learning machine” is 10 people doing roughly 2,500 management meetings a year, plus trade shows, suppliers, customers, competitors, and cross-country pattern matching.

- **AI is a multi-decade S-curve, but harder to size than mobile.** The stack: infrastructure first, then cloud delivery / neo-clouds / on-prem, then foundation models, then applications. Whale Rock preferred infrastructure first because demand is broad and winner analysis is more tractable.

- **AI capex is not done, in his view.** He says AI infrastructure penetration is only about 14% overall and 3–4% within cloud. Inference-time reasoning could increase compute intensity sharply because a single user query may trigger many model calls.

- **Shorting with the same S-curve lens.** Good shorts can be mature tech being disrupted or premature “false S-curves.” But he warns against shorting old tech too lazily: a melting ice cube can still last 15 years if the market has already priced it for death.

- **EVs were a useful mistake.** The EV S-curve worked in China, but US adoption hit a wall around 10%. When the S-curve went from “10/10” to “0/10,” and Tesla’s underappreciated earnings power was no longer cheap, Whale Rock exited.

- **Portfolio construction is concentrated.** They can buy up to 10% at cost and let names run to ~15%, usually larger and more liquid names. One major position at 10% is typical, with other high-conviction names sized below that.

- **Mag 7 concentration is not automatically a bubble.** Sacerdote argues it reflects digital-platform economics: leaders compound faster, accrue more profit, and have data/distribution advantages. He cites Amazon around 25x next-year GAAP EPS, Meta around 22x, Microsoft around 26x, while Tesla is higher.

- **Mag 7 may benefit from AI on both revenue and cost.** Advertising gets better targeting and automated creative; clouds monetize enterprise AI demand; software-heavy companies can apply AI to coding and digital workflows faster than analog businesses.

- **Other S-curves watched:** driverless taxis have reached early mainstream takeoff but are still tiny as a share of miles driven; robotics is exciting but likely slower than hype suggests for complex humanoid tasks.

- **Private markets matter because companies stay private longer.** Whale Rock uses the same public-market S-curve framework in late-stage privates. Key private holdings mentioned: Stripe, Canva, Databricks, Revolut.

- **Crypto take: mostly false/early, but stablecoins real.** He was skeptical of broad blockchain narratives as “technology looking for a problem,” not a Bitcoin buyer in the fund, but sees Bitcoin as digital-gold-like and stablecoins as a true S-curve for efficient money movement.

- **Volatility is structural to tech investing.** Every 7–10 years tech sells off hard, often followed by a new innovation cycle. The business requires LPs who understand the process and can tolerate drawdowns.

## Notable quotes

- “S-curve, competitive advantage, underappreciated earnings power.”
- “The S-curve framework gives you insight into how big the market can be... it’s like a map into the future.”
- “The biggest reason people blow up in tech is they miss on revenue.”
- “Are you an ice cube on a hot day in Arizona... or a block of ice in Massachusetts in 35-degree weather?”
- On AI infrastructure: “Invest in the infrastructure layer first because that’s always the first to inflect.”

## Actionable takeaways

- For tech investing, explicitly underwrite the adoption curve before underwriting the stock.
- Separate “big theme” from “investable timing”; being 10 years early can still be a bad trade.
- Look for companies where distribution, data, network effects, scale, or IP turn S-curve growth into durable earnings power.
- For AI, the cleaner near-term underwriting may still be infrastructure and cloud, not picking application winners too early.
- Treat Mag 7 valuation name-by-name; concentration alone is not proof of a bubble.
- Shorting disruption requires timing discipline: old tech can be cheap and cash-generative for much longer than expected.

## One-line summary

Sacerdote’s playbook: buy the few tech companies where a real adoption S-curve plus durable advantage makes future earnings power much bigger than today’s market model.
