# THIS Part of Singapore Feels Surprisingly Japanese

📺 Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ansdgsN1UoI

## TL;DR

Host explores Novena as a quiet “mini Japan town” in Singapore: not cosplay Japan, but practical expat infrastructure clustered around food, clinics, malls, and central location. Core point: Japanese residents likely choose Novena because daily-life friction is low — Japanese food, Japanese doctors, serviced apartments, hospitals, and quick access to Orchard.

## Key Points

- Novena = central but less flashy than Orchard/River Valley. Few MRT stops from Orchard, known by locals mainly for hospitals, but host sees many Japanese residents and Japanese-oriented businesses.
- Host sets two challenges: find at least 10 Japanese things, and see if he can “live like a Japanese person” there under S$100.
- Food density is main draw. First stop: Kamome Bakery, Japanese-run, with breads like mentai tamagoyaki and onion teriyaki. Host says it feels like Tokyo bakery culture.
- Don Don Donki differs by location. Novena branch has cheap items like soy milk around S$0.80 and 5-piece sushi around S$8.80.
- Sponsored segment: Weave Suites Novena. Fully furnished serviced apartments, all-inclusive rent covering utilities/Wi-Fi/amenities, housekeeping/linen changes, repairs, 24/7 CCTV/security, flexible short/long stays. Pitch is relocation, renovation waiting period, or testing area before committing.
- Healthcare cluster matters. Host notices National Neuroscience Institute, Chinese hospital, and many clinics/hospitals. His read: Japanese residents may feel safer and more comfortable near healthcare.
- Japanese clinic is key. He finds clinic with many Japanese-language reviews and frames it as expat trust infrastructure: if you lived in Tokyo and found Singaporean doctor/clinic, you might choose to live nearby.
- Retail cluster includes Daiso/Threeppy, Japan Home (not actually Japanese, “tries hard”), Japanese curry, yakiniku, sushi, Japanese hair-removal brand, Japanese hair salon, Mr. Donut.
- Dinner at an izakaya: menu has gyoza variants, yuzu pepper, curry, yakitori, udon, sushi, stamina don. Host says place doesn’t look fully authentic, but menu has “hardcore Japanese” cues.
- Food verdict mixed: red-pepper gyoza has good flavor but mushy texture; pork leek kushiyaki disappoints; pork enoki cheese better; stamina don close to Japan but could use more garlic; signature yakitori and chicken cartilage are strong, especially with beer.
- Final conclusion: Novena’s Japanese feel works because it is not aggressively themed. It “naturally became this way” through residents’ daily needs.

## Operator / Local Read-Through

- Novena moat = convenience stack, not tourist identity: central MRT, medical trust, Japanese food/retail, serviced housing, expat-friendly lifestyle.
- Good example of diaspora clustering: once food + healthcare + housing exist, more residents choose area, which supports more Japanese businesses.
- For Singapore property/lifestyle lens: Novena is not domestic-TAM exciting, but high-trust urban infrastructure makes it sticky for expats, medical tourists, and temporary residents.
- For operators: product wedge is not “Japan-themed mall”; it is reducing relocation friction — language, healthcare confidence, food familiarity, flexible housing.

## Notable Lines

- “Most people think hospitals when they think of Novena. But there’s actually a strong Japanese community living quietly.”
- “What makes this place special is that it’s not trying to be Japanese. It just naturally became this way.”

## Caveats

## One-Line Summary

Novena feels Japanese because it solves daily expat friction — food, clinics, housing, malls, hospitals — rather than because it is trying to be a tourist-style Japan town.
