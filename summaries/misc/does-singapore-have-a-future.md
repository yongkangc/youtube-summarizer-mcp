# Does Singapore Have a Future?

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E84avHDDWI8

## TL;DR

Max Chernov interviews Professor Arnoud De Meyer, former SMU president and former INSEAD dean, on Singapore’s future. De Meyer’s view: Singapore still has a strong future because it keeps renewing its economic base, has low corruption, high trust, strong regulation, and a stable regional position. Weaknesses: small domestic market, some self-restraint/risk aversion, and difficulty scaling Singapore-born businesses into messier regional markets.

## Core Points

- Singapore is no longer just “boring/sterile.”
  - De Meyer says that reputation came partly from visitors only seeing Orchard Road, hotels, and shopping malls.
  - Living inside local networks reveals more cultural activity, concerts, communities, and vibrancy than 20 years ago.
  - He thinks Singapore has fewer rules than many European countries; difference is that rules are enforced.

- Singapore’s constraint is not lack of capability, but small market size.
  - Domestic consumer market is roughly 4.5 million if excluding migrant workers and domestic helpers.
  - Entrepreneurs quickly need to think beyond Singapore.
  - ASEAN looks nearby, but rules/informal systems differ sharply from Singapore, making expansion hard.
  - US/Europe are larger but far away and highly competitive.

- Entrepreneurship has improved.
  - In early 2000s/2010s, young Singaporeans were more reluctant to become entrepreneurs.
  - Reasons: comfortable government/corporate path, parental preference for stable jobs, and strong safety/structure.
  - Over last 10–15 years, more young people are willing to take risk and start companies.

- Singapore’s real edge is renewal.
  - It moved from disk drives/Seagate-era manufacturing to petrochemicals, trade, AI, startups, and regional HQ functions.
  - De Meyer sees Singapore’s policy strength as constant reinvention around new industrial opportunities.
  - Current AI/company setup activity benefits from stable regulation, IP protection, infrastructure, and trust.

- Trust is Singapore’s premium product.
  - Low corruption, transparency, clear accounting, predictable tax/regulatory changes, stable government policy.
  - Useful for finance, pharma, medical tourism, and any business where counterparty trust matters.
  - De Meyer contrasts this with more volatile regional environments and says Singapore invoices/bank accounts carry trust premium.

- Universities still matter in AI world, but model changes.
  - He prefers “institutions of higher learning” because the university model may diversify.
  - Three key functions remain:
    - critical thinking and decision-making
    - network formation
    - safe experimentation with low downside
  - Undergraduate education should ideally happen where the student expects to build future life/network.

- AI changes education from theory delivery to application.
  - Theory/concepts can increasingly be downloaded or learned from videos/AI.
  - Students still need foundational principles and ability to distinguish good information from hallucination.
  - More value shifts to experiential learning: student + faculty mentor + practitioner working on real projects.

- Networks are not just introductions.
  - De Meyer frames networks as ways to compensate for weaknesses, acquire knowledge, get honest feedback, and mobilize resources.
  - Entrepreneurship is lonely; founders need trusted partners because entrepreneurship means mobilizing resources you do not control.
  - He uses Coase/Williamson transaction-cost theory: organizations exist because pure market contracting is expensive; trust and hierarchy reduce coordination cost.

- Advice to young people: take opportunities.
  - De Meyer describes himself as optimistic: “glass usually two-thirds full.”
  - He has seen failures, including ventures that had to be shut down/sold for symbolic value.
  - His advice: do not forgo opportunities, accept risk, do work you like, and keep moving after failure.

- Geopolitics may raise Singapore’s attractiveness.
  - He argues Gulf instability damages the trust/safety premium of Dubai/Abu Dhabi-style hubs.
  - Singapore benefits from ASEAN stability and perception of predictability.
  - He thinks China may be a long-run beneficiary of Middle East conflict because it looks stable/predictable and has built oil reserves.
  - Global South and some European countries may move closer to China as hedge against unpredictable US policy.

- Democracy: he favors stable social democracy, not wild swings.
  - De Meyer supports coalition-style systems that allow voter feedback while avoiding extreme policy oscillation.
  - Singapore elections also serve as policy signal; he cites 2011 PAP vote loss as a moment when government adjusted policies.

- Europe’s mistake: welfare expectations without growth.
  - Europeans expect expanding social safety net: unemployment, health, education, culture.
  - High taxes fund it, but the model hits limits without growth and with aging demographics.
  - Europe has used immigration to offset demographics, but integration/social absorptive capacity has limits.
  - Europe needs more productivity, innovation, and growth.
  - He contrasts weak European innovation with China’s EV progress and US/China dominance in AI companies.

## Investor / Operator Read-Through

- Singapore’s moat is institutional trust, not cheapness or market size.
  - Good for HQs, finance, healthcare, pharma, IP-sensitive tech, regional governance, and premium services.
  - Bad for consumer startups needing massive home-market scale unless they can expand regionally fast.

- Singapore remains a strong base, but not enough as market.
  - Build from Singapore when trust/regulatory premium matters.
  - Underwrite expansion separately country by country; do not assume “ASEAN” is one scalable market.

- Europe read-through: secular pressure unless productivity improves.
  - Welfare state + aging + low fertility + weak innovation = fiscal compression.
  - Europe’s equity opportunity is selective innovation/export champions, not broad dynamism.

- AI education read-through: universities that only deliver lectures lose value; universities that provide networks, judgment, and real-world projects gain value.

## Best Lines / Ideas

- Singapore has fewer rules than some European countries; it just enforces them.
- Entrepreneurship is mobilizing resources you do not control.
- Universities are places to experiment without severe negative consequences.
- Europe needs growth because redistribution requires something to redistribute.
- Meaning of life: being part of a community, helping and being helped.

## One-Line Summary

Singapore’s future is not in being a huge market; it is in being a high-trust, low-corruption, constantly renewing base for capital, talent, companies, and regional coordination.
