## The Interconnection Crisis: 70:1 and Getting Worse

Here's a number that should stop you cold: **78 gigawatts**.

That's the total datacenter load currently sitting in ERCOT's interconnection queue in Texas alone. The amount that's actually been approved and connected? **1.1 gigawatts**.

That's a 70:1 ratio. For every megawatt of AI datacenter capacity that gets grid power, 70 megawatts are waiting in line.

The average time to get through that queue? **5 to 7 years**.

Now consider the demand trajectory. AI-specific datacenter power consumption was approximately 3 GW in 2023. By 2026, it's projected to hit 28 GW. By 2030? **110 GW**.

That's a 67% compound annual growth rate. The grid is not designed for this. It was built for gradual, predictable load growth of 1-2% annually. We're asking it to absorb load growth that's 30-50x faster than historical norms.

The result is a catastrophic mismatch between supply and demand. And in that mismatch lies one of the most obvious—yet most overlooked—arbitrage opportunities in the market today.

## The $5.2 Billion Arbitrage: A Simple Math Problem

Let me walk you through the economics that are driving every major AI infrastructure decision right now.

Consider a 200 MW datacenter—a modest facility by today's hyperscaler standards. At current market rates, that facility generates approximately **$11 million per MW per year** in revenue. That's $2.2 billion in annual revenue for a 200 MW site.

**Scenario A: Wait for Grid Power**

You file your interconnection request. You wait through feasibility studies, system impact studies, facility studies. You negotiate with the utility. You wait for transmission upgrades. Best case? You're operational in **Month 36**.

For the first three years, your $2 billion datacenter sits dark. Your GPUs depreciate. Your competitors train models. Your customers defect.

**Scenario B: Bring Your Own Generation (BYOG)**

You order natural gas turbines. You permit on private land (avoiding the worst regulatory delays). You install, commission, and start generating power by **Month 6**.

You're earning $2.2 billion annually while your grid-dependent competitors are still waiting for study results.

The **36-month cumulative profit difference**: approximately **$5.2 billion**.

Read that again. The decision to generate your own power versus waiting for the grid creates a **$5.2 billion advantage** over three years for a single 200 MW facility.

This isn't a rounding error. This is the entire economics of AI infrastructure.

**Speed is the only moat.** The companies that recognized this first—xAI, Crusoe, Oracle—are building the future of AI. The companies waiting in queue are building stranded assets.

## The Technology Stack: What Actually Works

For investors trying to understand this space, the technology landscape can seem overwhelming. Let me simplify it.

There are three primary paths to BYOG power, each with distinct economics and use cases:

### 1. Aeroderivative Gas Turbines
**Key Products:** GE LM2500, LM6000
**Capital Cost:** $1,700-2,000/kW
**Deployment Timeline:** 18-36 months
**Best For:** Bridge power and medium-term installations

These are jet engines modified to generate electricity. They're proven, reliable, and relatively fast to deploy. GE Vernova's LM2500XPRESS—a mobile, containerized version—can be operational in as little as 12-18 months.

The key advantage: **fast ramp rates**. These turbines can go from zero to full power in minutes, making them ideal for AI workloads with variable demand.

### 2. Fuel Cells
**Key Products:** Bloom Energy servers
**Capital Cost:** $3,000-4,000/kW
**Deployment Timeline:** 3-12 months
**Best For:** Permitting-constrained urban sites

Here's something most investors miss: the fastest path to power isn't always the cheapest hardware. It's the hardware that avoids regulatory bottlenecks.

Bloom Energy's solid oxide fuel cells have **no combustion**. That means no air quality permits. No EPA New Source Review. No multi-year environmental impact studies.

In dense urban areas where air permits can take 2-3 years, fuel cells can be operational in **3-12 months**. The higher CapEx is irrelevant when the alternative is years of delay.

### 3. Combined Cycle Gas Turbines (CCGT)
**Key Products:** GE HA.02, Doosan DGT6
**Capital Cost:** $800-1,200/kW
**Deployment Timeline:** 24-36 months
**Best For:** Permanent large-scale installations

For gigawatt-scale permanent facilities, H-class combined cycle plants offer the best long-term economics. They're more efficient than aeroderivatives, with heat rates below 6,000 BTU/kWh.

The trade-off is timeline. A full CCGT installation takes 24-36 months. But for facilities planning 20-year operational horizons, the efficiency gains compound dramatically.

### The Cost Reality

Let me address the obvious question: isn't onsite generation more expensive than grid power?

Yes. Grid power in competitive markets costs approximately **$60/MWh**. Onsite natural gas generation runs **$65-91/MWh** depending on technology and gas prices.

But this comparison misses the point entirely.

**You can't buy grid power that doesn't exist.** The question isn't "grid vs. gas"—it's "gas now vs. nothing for five years."

When the alternative is zero revenue, paying a 10-50% premium for power is the obvious choice. The speed premium more than compensates for the cost differential.

### STRONG BUY: Howmet Aerospace (HWM)

**Market Cap:** ~$50B
**Position:** Near-monopoly on high-temperature turbine blade castings

This is the hidden monopoly most investors have never heard of.

Every GE turbine, every Siemens turbine, every Mitsubishi turbine—they all require turbine blades. And not just any blades. The blades in high-efficiency gas turbines must withstand temperatures above 1,400°C while spinning at 3,600 RPM.

Making these blades requires **single-crystal nickel superalloy casting**—one of the most technically demanding manufacturing processes in existence. The blade is grown as a single crystal, with no grain boundaries, allowing it to operate at temperatures that would instantly destroy conventional metallurgy.

Howmet Aerospace, along with Precision Castparts (owned by Berkshire Hathaway), controls virtually all Western production of these components.

Here's the key insight: **you cannot build a gas turbine without these blades**. GE Vernova can announce all the orders they want—they can only deliver turbines as fast as Howmet can supply blade castings.

The supply chain works like this:
- AI demand drives turbine orders
- Turbine orders drive blade casting demand
- Blade casting capacity is the binding constraint

Every 1 GW of gas turbines sold translates directly to Howmet revenue. As BYOG buildout accelerates, Howmet's order book fills regardless of which turbine OEM wins.

The blades require rare earth elements—rhenium, yttrium, cobalt—that are geopolitically constrained. Howmet's existing casting lines and materials relationships create a moat that's nearly impossible to replicate.

**Thesis:** Howmet is the NVIDIA of turbine blades. A near-monopoly on a critical component in a demand supercycle. The market treats it as an aerospace supplier. It's actually an AI infrastructure company.

## The Supply Chain Constraint: Turbine Blades Are the New GPUs

Here's the second-order insight that few investors have internalized:

**The binding constraint on BYOG buildout is not turbine manufacturing capacity. It's turbine blade casting capacity.**

Modern gas turbines require single-crystal nickel superalloy blades that can withstand temperatures above the melting point of the alloy itself (through internal cooling channels and thermal barrier coatings). Manufacturing these blades requires:

1. **Single-crystal casting expertise** - The blade is directionally solidified as a single crystal, eliminating grain boundaries that would cause failure
2. **Rare earth elements** - Rhenium, yttrium, hafnium, and cobalt are essential alloying elements
3. **Specialized coating facilities** - Thermal barrier coatings require electron beam physical vapor deposition

Western capacity for this manufacturing is concentrated in Howmet Aerospace and Precision Castparts (Berkshire). Chinese suppliers exist but face quality concerns and potential export restrictions.

The rare earth supply chain adds another layer of risk. China controls 60%+ of global rare earth processing. Rhenium—critical for blade performance—is primarily produced as a byproduct of molybdenum mining, with supply concentrated in Chile and a few other nations.

**Every 1 GW of gas turbine orders creates derivative demand for blade castings, rare earth elements, and specialized coatings.** Following this supply chain upstream reveals investable opportunities beyond the turbine OEMs themselves.

## Risk Factors: What Could Break the Thesis

No investment thesis is complete without acknowledging what could go wrong.

### 1. Natural Gas Price Spike

The economics of BYOG assume natural gas prices in the $3-5/MMBtu range. If gas spikes above **$10/MMBtu**—as it did briefly in 2022—the cost advantage of speed diminishes significantly.

At $10/MMBtu, onsite generation costs rise to $120-150/MWh, making grid power (where available) substantially cheaper. A sustained gas price spike would pressure BYOG economics and potentially slow deployment.

**Mitigation:** Long-term gas supply contracts and hedge positions can lock in economics. Many BYOG deployments are structuring 5-10 year fixed-price gas supply agreements.

### 2. FERC Order 2023 Accelerates Grid Interconnection

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission's Order 2023, issued in 2023 with implementation ongoing, aims to accelerate interconnection processes. If successful, it could reduce queue times from 5-7 years to 2-3 years.

Faster grid interconnection would reduce the speed premium that justifies BYOG economics.

**Mitigation:** Even optimistic projections suggest Order 2023 impact will take 3-5 years to materialize. The current queue backlog doesn't disappear overnight. BYOG demand runway extends regardless.

### 3. Chip Efficiency Gains Reduce Power Demand

Morgan Stanley has projected that AI chip efficiency could improve by **10,000x** over the coming decade as process nodes shrink and architectures optimize.

If true, the MW-per-model requirement could decline dramatically, reducing total power demand.

**Mitigation:** Historical patterns suggest efficiency gains are typically offset by capability expansion. More efficient chips enable larger models and more inference volume, maintaining or increasing total power demand. But this remains a risk worth monitoring.

### 4. Chinese Rare Earth Export Controls

If geopolitical tensions escalate, China could restrict exports of rare earth elements critical to turbine blade manufacturing.

This would constrain BYOG supply chains and potentially slow the buildout.

**Mitigation:** Diversification of rare earth processing is underway in the US, Australia, and elsewhere. Howmet and PCC have been building inventory positions. But short-term supply disruptions are possible.
